Section E1A.

It is not legal to transmit a 3 kilohertz bandwidth upper side band signal with a carrier frequency of 14.348 megahertz on 20 meters because the upper 1 kilohertz of the signal extends beyond the band edge at 14.350 megahertz. Notes: upper side band voice signals extend from the carrier frequency upward by their bandwidth, so a 3 kilohertz upper side band signal at 14.348 megahertz occupies 14.348 to 14.351 megahertz. This causes the top 1 kilohertz of the signal to fall outside the authorized 20-meter band, making the transmission illegal.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

When using a transceiver that displays the carrier frequency of phone signals, the lowest frequency at which a properly adjusted lower side band emission will be totally within the band is 3 kilohertz above the lower band edge. Notes: An lower side band phone signal occupies bandwidth extending above the displayed carrier frequency, so the carrier must be set high enough to keep the entire sideband inside the band. A 3 kilohertz offset above the lower band edge ensures that a typical 3 kilohertz-wide lower side band signal remains completely within the authorized frequency range.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The highest legal carrier frequency on the 20-meter band for transmitting a 2.8 kilohertz wide upper side band data signal is 14.1472 megahertz. Notes: The upper band edge for 20 meters is 14.150 megahertz, and FCC rules require that the entire transmitted signal, including its 2.8 kilohertz bandwidth, remain within the band. Subtracting 2.8 kilohertz from 14.150 megahertz results in a maximum carrier frequency of 14.1472 megahertz.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An Extra class operator may not answer the CQ of a station on 3.601 megahertz lower side band phone because the sideband components will extend beyond the edge of the 75-meter phone band segment. Notes: In lower side band phone operation, the occupied bandwidth extends downward from the suppressed carrier frequency, so transmitting on 3.601 megahertz would cause part of the signal to fall outside the authorized phone subband. FCC rules require that all transmitted energy remain within the designated phone band segment.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Any person holding an FCC issued amateur license or who is authorized for alien reciprocal operation must be in physical control of the station apparatus of an amateur station aboard any vessel or craft that is documented or registered in the United States. Notes: FCC rules require that amateur stations on U.S.-documented or registered vessels be controlled by someone who is legally authorized to operate under U.S. amateur service regulations. This includes licensed U.S. amateurs and foreign amateurs operating under valid reciprocal operating authority.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

For channelized 60 meter operation using CW, the transmitter must be set to the exact center frequency of the assigned channel. Notes: On the 60 meter band, FCC rules require all emissions, including CW, to be centered in the assigned channel to avoid interference and maintain the channelized structure. Tuning to the channel center frequency ensures compliance with the band’s specific channel-based allocation.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The maximum power permitted on the 2200-meter band is 1 watt equivalent isotropic radiated power (EIRP). Notes: FCC rules limit amateur operation on the 2200-meter band to 1 watt EIRP to minimize potential interference to other services using this low-frequency spectrum. EIRP includes both transmitter power and antenna gain, so amateurs must ensure the total radiated power does not exceed this 1 watt limit.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If a station in a message forwarding system inadvertently forwards a message that violates FCC rules, the control operator of the originating station is primarily accountable for the rules violation. Notes: FCC rules place primary responsibility for message content on the control operator of the station that originates the transmission. In a forwarding network, this means the originating station’s control operator is held chiefly accountable for any rule violations in the message.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Except in some parts of Alaska, the maximum power permitted on the 630-meter band is 5 watts EIRP. Notes: The FCC limits amateur operation on the 630-meter band to 5 watts EIRP in most U.S. locations to control interference and ensure compatibility with other services. EIRP accounts for both transmitter output power and antenna gain, so operators must calculate their system to stay within this 5-watt EIRP limit.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If an amateur station is installed aboard a ship or aircraft, its operation must be approved by the master of the ship or the pilot in command of the aircraft. Notes: The FCC requires that the person in overall command of the vessel or aircraft authorize amateur station operation to ensure safety and operational control. This keeps radio use coordinated with the ship’s or aircraft’s primary communications and safety responsibilities.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

When operating an amateur station aboard a US-registered vessel in international waters, any FCC-issued amateur license is sufficient authorization. Notes: For a US-registered vessel, FCC amateur service rules apply regardless of location, including international waters. Therefore, any valid FCC-issued amateur license grants the operator permission to use an amateur station on board.

Section E1B.

A spurious emission is an emission outside the signal’s necessary bandwidth that can be reduced or eliminated without affecting the information transmitted. Notes: In amateur radio, any signal energy that lies outside the bandwidth needed to carry the intended information and can be removed without degrading that information is considered spurious. Spurious emissions are unwanted and must be limited to comply with FCC technical standards and prevent interference to other users.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An acceptable bandwidth for digital voice or slow-scan TV transmissions made on the HF amateur bands is 3 kilohertz. Notes: On the HF amateur bands, digital voice and slow-scan TV signals are expected to fit within a typical single side band channel, which is about 3 kilohertz wide. This bandwidth limit helps prevent interference and promotes efficient spectrum use as required by FCC rules for HF operation.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An amateur station must protect an FCC monitoring facility from harmful interference if it is located within 1 mile of that facility. Notes: FCC monitoring stations perform sensitive signal measurements, so nearby amateur stations must avoid causing harmful interference. The 1 mile distance defines the protected zone around these facilities in the Amateur Extra rules.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If a radiolocation system experiences interference from a repeater operating in the 70-centimeter band, the control operator must cease operation or make changes to the repeater that mitigate the interference. Notes: In the 70-centimeter band, amateur repeaters are secondary users and must not cause harmful interference to primary services such as radiolocation. Therefore, the control operator is required to stop operation or modify the repeater so that the interference is resolved.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The National Radio Quiet Zone is an area surrounding the National Radio Astronomy Observatory. Notes: Radio emissions are restricted in the National Radio Quiet Zone to protect the sensitive observations made by the National Radio Astronomy Observatory. This helps minimize radio frequency interference to important radio astronomy research facilities located there.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If you are erecting an amateur station antenna structure at a site at or near a public use airport, you may have to notify the Federal Aviation Administration and register it with the FCC as required by Part 17 of the FCC rules. Notes: Antenna structures near public use airports can pose potential hazards to air navigation, so the FAA and FCC require notice and registration under Part 17. This ensures the structure is properly evaluated, marked, and recorded for aviation safety and regulatory compliance.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

PRB-1 applies to state and local zoning regulations that affect amateur radio antenna structures. Notes: PRB-1 is an FCC policy requiring state and local governments to reasonably accommodate amateur radio antennas in their zoning and land-use regulations. It specifically addresses how these non-federal authorities regulate the placement and height of amateur radio antenna structures.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If an amateur station with a properly operating transmitter causes interference to domestic broadcast reception in receivers of good engineering design, the FCC may require that the amateur station avoid transmitting during certain hours on the specific frequencies that cause the interference. Notes: The FCC can limit when an amateur station may operate on particular frequencies to prevent harmful interference to properly designed broadcast receivers. This protects domestic broadcast reception while still allowing amateur operation at other times or on other frequencies.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Under RACES rules, any FCC-licensed amateur station certified by the responsible civil defense organization for the area served may be operated. Notes: RACES operation is restricted to stations that are both FCC-licensed and specifically certified by the local civil defense authority. This ensures that emergency communications are coordinated and authorized within the served area during emergencies and civil defense activities.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Under RACES rules, an amateur station may operate on all amateur service frequencies that are authorized to the control operator. Notes: RACES operation does not grant additional frequency privileges, so the station is limited to the same amateur bands and segments the control operator is already authorized to use. This ensures emergency communications stay within the operator’s existing license class privileges and FCC regulations.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

PRB-1 requires that state and local regulations affecting amateur radio antenna size and structures must make reasonable accommodations for amateur radio communications and must not preclude amateur service communications altogether. Notes: Under PRB-1, the FCC directs state and local governments to reasonably accommodate amateur radio operations when regulating antennas. This protects the ability of licensed amateurs to communicate while still allowing local authorities to enforce legitimate zoning and safety regulations.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Section E1C.

The maximum bandwidth for a data emission on the 60 meter amateur band is 2.8 kilohertz. Notes: On 60 meters, the FCC limits data emissions to a maximum bandwidth of 2.8 kilohertz to ensure compatibility and prevent interference within the shared channels. Amateur Extra operators must observe this 2.8 kilohertz limit when configuring digital modes on 60 meters.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Communications transmitted to amateur stations in foreign countries must be limited to those incidental to the purpose of the amateur service and remarks of a personal nature. Notes: FCC rules and international agreements restrict third-party and international amateur communications to content that supports the amateur service’s technical and operational purposes plus personal, noncommercial conversation. This maintains the noncommercial, self-training, and hobby nature of amateur radio across borders.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An amateur operator may begin operating on the 2200-meter or 630-meter band 30 days after filing a notification with the Utilities Technology Council (UTC), provided they have not been informed that their station is within 1 kilometer of PLC systems using those frequencies. Notes: The 30-day waiting period allows time for the UTC to determine whether nearby power line carrier (PLC) systems could be affected. If no notice of being within 1 kilometer of PLC operations on those frequencies is received, operation on 2200 meters or 630 meters may begin.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An International Amateur Radio Permit (IARP) is a permit that allows U.S. amateur radio operators to operate their stations in certain countries of the Americas without obtaining an individual reciprocal license from each country. Notes: The IARP is an agreed-upon permit among certain nations in the Americas that recognizes a U.S. amateur’s operating privileges when traveling. It simplifies temporary operation abroad by serving as a standardized authorization in participating countries.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A station may transmit third party communications while being automatically controlled only when transmitting RTTY or data emissions. Notes: FCC rules for automatically controlled stations restrict third party communications to RTTY or data modes to ensure predictable, regulated operation without a control operator present in real time. This limitation helps maintain control and accountability for message content in unattended digital operation on the amateur bands.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

To operate in accordance with CEPT rules in foreign countries where permitted, you must have a copy of FCC Public Notice DA 16-1048. Notes: FCC Public Notice DA 16-1048 implements the CEPT amateur radio license arrangements for U.S. amateurs and serves as your proof of operating authority when visiting participating countries. Carrying a copy of this document shows foreign regulators that you are authorized to operate under CEPT rules.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Before transmitting on the 630- or 2200-meter amateur bands, operators must inform the Utilities Technology Council (UTC) of their call sign and the coordinates of the station. Notes: Notification to the Utilities Technology Council (UTC) with your call sign and station coordinates is required so that potential interference to power line carrier systems and utility infrastructure can be evaluated. This coordination step is part of the FCC rules specific to operation on the 630- and 2200-meter bands.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If the control link of a remotely controlled amateur station malfunctions, the maximum permissible duration of the station’s transmissions is 3 minutes. Notes: FCC rules limit a remotely controlled station to no more than 3 minutes of transmitting after control is lost to prevent unchecked, potentially interfering signals. This time limit helps ensure that malfunctioning or uncontrolled transmissions do not continue indefinitely on the air.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

For angle modulation emissions below 29.0 megahertz, the highest permitted modulation index at the highest modulation frequency is 1.0. Notes: FCC rules limit the modulation index for angle modulation below 29.0 megahertz to 1.0 at the highest modulation frequency to control occupied bandwidth and interference. This ensures the signal remains within its authorized emission limits for Amateur Extra operators.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The maximum mean power level for a spurious emission below 30 megahertz is -43 dB with respect to the fundamental emission. Notes: FCC rules limit spurious emissions to -43 dB below the carrier to prevent interference on adjacent and nearby frequencies. This ensures that unwanted emissions are much weaker than the intended signal while operating below 30 megahertz.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The operating arrangement that allows an FCC-licensed US citizen to operate in many European countries, and amateurs from many European countries to operate in the US, is the CEPT agreement. Notes: CEPT is an international reciprocal operating agreement that standardizes temporary amateur radio operating privileges among member countries. Under CEPT, eligible US and European amateurs can legally operate in each other’s countries without obtaining an additional local amateur license.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In the 630-meter band, phone emissions are permitted over the entire band. Notes: FCC rules allow voice (phone) operation anywhere within the authorized 630-meter amateur band segment. This means there is no sub-band restriction for phone emissions on 630 meters for Amateur Extra licensees.

Section E1D.

Telemetry is defined as the one-way transmission of measurements at a distance from the measuring instrument. Notes: In the Amateur Extra exam context, telemetry refers specifically to sending measurement or status data in one direction from the remote instrument back to a control or monitoring point. This definition distinguishes it from two-way control or voice communications.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Telecommand signals from a space telecommand station may transmit encrypted messages under the FCC Amateur Extra rules. Notes: The FCC rules specifically allow encryption when it is used for telecommand of a space station or radio control from a space telecommand station. This exception permits secure control of satellites while maintaining compliance with amateur service regulations.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A space telecommand station is an amateur station that transmits communications to initiate, modify, or terminate functions of a space station. Notes: In the Amateur Extra exam context, a space telecommand station specifically controls the operation of an amateur space station by sending signals that start, change, or stop its functions. This definition aligns with FCC rules governing amateur operation of satellites and other space-based stations.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Identification transmissions from a balloon-borne telemetry station must include the station's FCC-assigned call sign. Notes: The FCC requires all amateur stations, including balloon-borne telemetry stations, to identify with their call sign so the transmitting station can be clearly and uniquely identified. Including the call sign in the telemetry identification satisfies this regulatory requirement for proper station identification on the Amateur Extra exam.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

When a station is being operated by telecommand on or within 50 kilometers of the Earth’s surface, the posting at the station location must include the FCC licensee’s name, the station call sign, and the address and telephone number of the station licensee or designated control point, since all these choices are correct. Notes: For telecommanded stations near the Earth's surface, the rules require clear identification of the licensee and how to reach the control point. Listing the name, call sign, and contact information ensures accountability and the ability to promptly control or shut down the station if necessary.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The maximum permitted transmitter output power when operating a model craft by telecommand is 1 watt. Notes: FCC rules limit telecommand of model craft to a maximum transmitter output power of 1 watt to minimize interference and ensure safe shared use of the spectrum. Amateur Extra operators must observe this specific power cap when controlling model craft by radio.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The HF amateur bands that include allocations for space stations are 40 meters, 20 meters, 15 meters, and 10 meters. Notes: The FCC allocates specific segments within the 40-meter, 20-meter, 15-meter, and 10-meter HF bands for amateur space station operation. Knowing which HF bands permit space station use is necessary for proper band planning and regulatory compliance on the Amateur Extra exam.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

On the 2-meter VHF amateur band, specific frequencies are authorized for use by amateur space stations. Notes: The 2-meter band includes designated segments where amateur space stations are permitted to operate under FCC rules. This makes 2 meters the VHF amateur band with authorized space station frequencies for the Amateur Extra exam context.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The UHF amateur bands that have frequencies authorized for space stations are 70 centimeters and 13 centimeters. Notes: These two UHF bands include specific frequency segments allocated by the FCC and the ITU for amateur satellite and space station operation. Knowing which bands support space stations is essential for properly planning and licensing satellite communications in the Amateur Extra service.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Any amateur station so designated by the space station licensee is eligible to be the telecommand station of an amateur space station, subject to the privileges of the class of operator license held by the control operator. Notes: The space station licensee has the authority to designate which amateur stations may serve as its telecommand stations. Those stations must still operate within the operating privileges of the control operator’s license class under FCC rules for the Amateur Extra exam context.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Any amateur station is eligible to operate as an Earth station, subject to the privileges of the class of operator license held by the control operator. Notes: Under FCC Amateur Extra rules, Earth station operation is just another authorized operating mode and is allowed to any amateur station as long as the control operator stays within their license class frequency and privilege limits. The license class determines where and how that Earth station operation may occur on the amateur bands, not whether it is allowed at all.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

One-way communications are permitted from an amateur space station, beacon station, or telecommand station. Notes: These specific station types are allowed to transmit one-way signals because they support functions like satellite operation, propagation and activity monitoring, or remote control under FCC Amateur Extra rules. Other amateur stations are generally intended for two-way communications and do not have this blanket one-way transmission allowance.

Section E1E.

Under Part 97 rules, Volunteer Examiners (VEs) and Volunteer Examiner Coordinators (VECs) may be reimbursed for out-of-pocket expenses directly related to preparing, processing, administering, and coordinating an examination for an amateur radio operator license. Notes: The FCC allows VEs and VECs to recover only their actual expenses incurred in running amateur license exams. This keeps the volunteer examination system non-commercial while still covering necessary exam-related costs.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In the United States, Part 97 assigns the responsibility for maintaining the pools of questions for all amateur radio license examinations to the Volunteer Examiner Coordinators (VECs). Notes: Under FCC Part 97 rules, the Volunteer Examiner Coordinators are formally designated to develop, organize, and maintain the official question pools for all amateur license exam elements. This centralized responsibility helps ensure consistency and regulatory compliance across all exam sessions nationwide.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A Volunteer Examiner Coordinator is an organization that has entered into an agreement with the FCC to coordinate, prepare, and administer amateur operator license examinations. Notes: In the FCC amateur radio licensing system, Volunteer Examiner Coordinators manage and oversee the volunteer examiners who give license exams. Their agreement with the FCC authorizes them to ensure exams are prepared, coordinated, and administered according to FCC rules.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

To be accredited as a Volunteer Examiner, a Volunteer Examiner Coordinator must confirm that the VE applicant meets all FCC requirements to serve as an examiner. Notes: Accreditation as a VE is granted only after a VEC verifies that the applicant satisfies the FCC’s eligibility standards for examiners. This ensures that only qualified and compliant individuals administer amateur radio license exams.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If an examinee does not pass the exam, the Volunteer Examiner (VE) team must return the application document to the examinee. Notes: FCC Amateur Extra exam procedures require that when an exam is failed, no application is filed and the original application form is given back to the candidate. This ensures incomplete or unsuccessful exam attempts are not submitted for licensing action.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Each administering VE is responsible for the proper conduct and necessary supervision during an amateur operator license examination session. Notes: FCC rules assign responsibility for examination session conduct and supervision to each Volunteer Examiner who administers the test. This ensures accountability for exam integrity and compliance with Part 97 requirements.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If a candidate fails to comply with the examiner’s instructions during an amateur operator license examination, the Volunteer Examiner (VE) must immediately terminate the candidate’s examination. Notes: Examinations must be conducted under strict, orderly conditions to ensure fairness and integrity. Terminating the exam when a candidate does not follow instructions protects the validity of the test session and maintains FCC compliance.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A Volunteer Examiner (VE) may not administer an amateur radio license examination to any relative specified in the FCC rules. Notes: FCC rules prohibit VEs from examining certain relatives to avoid conflicts of interest and ensure exam integrity. This maintains fairness and impartiality in the amateur radio licensing process.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The penalty for a VE who fraudulently administers or certifies an examination may be revocation of the VE’s amateur station license grant and the suspension of the VE’s amateur operator license grant. Notes: The FCC can revoke the VE’s station license and suspend their operator license to protect the integrity of the amateur radio licensing system. This strong penalty discourages fraud in the examination process and maintains trust in issued licenses.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

After the administration of a successful examination for an amateur operator license, the administering VEs must submit the application document to the coordinating VEC according to the coordinating VEC instructions. Notes: Volunteer Examiners are required by FCC rules to forward successful exam results through their coordinating VEC. Following the coordinating VEC’s specific submission instructions ensures the license application is properly processed and entered into the FCC system.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

If an examinee earns a passing grade on all examination elements required for an upgrade or new amateur radio license, three volunteer examiners (VEs) must certify that the examinee is qualified for the license grant and that they have complied with all administering VE requirements. Notes: FCC rules require that at least three VEs formally attest to both the examinee’s qualification and the proper administration of the exam. This certification is what authorizes the FCC to issue the new or upgraded Amateur Extra license.

Section E1F.

Spread spectrum transmissions are permitted only on amateur frequencies above 222 megahertz. Notes: FCC rules restrict amateur spread spectrum operation to bands above 222 megahertz to limit interference and ensure compatibility with existing narrowband services. For the Amateur Extra exam, remember that spread spectrum is only allowed on amateur frequencies higher than 222 megahertz.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In the United States, a person holding an amateur service license granted by the government of Canada is authorized to operate under the terms and conditions of their Canadian amateur service license, but their privileges may not exceed those of a US Amateur Extra class license. Notes: Under the reciprocal operating agreement, Canadian amateurs in the US follow the operating terms of their Canadian license, but their maximum privileges are capped at the US Amateur Extra class level. This ensures foreign-licensed operators do not receive operating authority beyond the highest US amateur class while in US territory.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A dealer may sell an external RF power amplifier capable of operation below 144 megahertz if it has not been granted FCC certification only when the amplifier is constructed or modified by an amateur radio operator for use at an amateur station. Notes: FCC rules allow the sale of a non-certified external RF power amplifier below 144 megahertz only when it is built or modified by a licensed amateur for use at an amateur station. This exception supports legitimate amateur experimentation while still restricting uncertified amplifiers in general commerce.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

"Line A" is a line roughly parallel to and south of the border between the US and Canada. Notes: In the FCC rules, Line A is a regulatory boundary defined near and south of the US-Canada border. It is used to coordinate certain radio operations and protect stations near that international border region.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Amateur stations located in the contiguous 48 states and north of Line A may not transmit in the 420 megahertz to 430 megahertz frequency segment. Notes: The 420 megahertz to 430 megahertz segment is restricted in this region to protect primary users, so amateur operation there is prohibited. This limitation is part of FCC rules that define where amateurs may legally transmit in the 70-centimeter band near Line A.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The FCC might issue a Special Temporary Authority (STA) to an amateur station to provide for experimental amateur communications. Notes: An STA allows the FCC to temporarily authorize operations that support experimentation beyond normal rules. This flexibility supports the Amateur Extra service’s role in advancing radio art and technical investigation.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An amateur station may send a message to a business only when neither the amateur nor their employer has any pecuniary interest in the communications. Notes: FCC rules prohibit amateur radio communications made for pay or other financial benefit, so messages to a business are allowed only when no one involved has a monetary interest in the communication. This preserves the non-commercial nature of the Amateur Radio Service for all license classes, including Amateur Extra.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Amateur station communications transmitted for hire or material compensation are prohibited, except as otherwise specifically allowed by the FCC rules. Notes: FCC regulations prohibit using an amateur station for communications made for payment or other material gain. Limited exceptions exist in the rules, but in general amateur radio is a non-commercial service and must not be used for hire or compensation.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Messages encoded to obscure their meaning cannot be transmitted over an amateur radio mesh network. Notes: FCC rules prohibit using codes or ciphers intended to hide the meaning of messages on amateur radio, including mesh networks. All amateur communications must remain open and understandable to comply with transparency requirements for the Amateur Extra service.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Only Technician, General, Advanced, or Amateur Extra class operators may be the control operator of an auxiliary station. Notes: FCC rules require that the control operator of an auxiliary station hold at least a Technician class license. This ensures that the operator has the necessary privileges and knowledge to properly control unattended or remotely controlled transmitting equipment used in auxiliary operation.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

To qualify for a grant of FCC certification, an external RF power amplifier must satisfy the FCC’s spurious emission standards when operated at the lesser of 1500 watts or its full output power. Notes: FCC certification requires that external RF power amplifiers limit unwanted emissions to protect other spectrum users. Testing at the lesser of 1500 watts or the amplifier’s full output power ensures compliance under maximum normal operating conditions for amateur stations.

Section E2A.

The direction of an ascending pass for an amateur satellite is from south to north. Notes: An ascending pass is defined as the portion of the satellite’s orbit where it moves from the Southern Hemisphere toward the Northern Hemisphere as seen from Earth. For an observer, this corresponds to the satellite traveling generally from south to north across the sky.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

An inverting linear transponder produces an output signal that is the conjugate of the input, reverses the sideband orientation (upper to lower and lower to upper), and reverses the frequency spectrum of the transmitted passband, so all these choices are correct. Notes: Each listed behavior describes how an inverting linear transponder processes signals in amateur satellite operation. Because it inverts both sideband sense and passband frequency while maintaining linearity, all of the provided statements are simultaneously true.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In an inverting linear transponder, the upload signal is mixed with a local oscillator signal and the difference product is transmitted. Notes: An inverting linear transponder uses frequency mixing so that the uplink signal is converted to a different frequency by subtracting it from a local oscillator signal. This difference product is then amplified and retransmitted, preserving the modulation while shifting the frequency and inverting the spectrum as used in satellite and similar amateur applications.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

The “mode” of an amateur radio satellite is the designation of its uplink and downlink frequency bands. Notes: In amateur satellite operation, the mode label tells operators which frequency band is used for transmitting to the satellite (uplink) and which band is used for receiving from it (downlink). This lets operators choose appropriate equipment and frequencies for successful communication through the satellite.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In a satellite’s mode designator, the letters specify the uplink and downlink frequency ranges. Notes: Each letter in the satellite mode (such as Mode U/V or L/S) corresponds to a specific amateur band used for the uplink and the downlink. This lets operators know which frequency ranges to use when communicating through that satellite.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Keplerian elements are parameters that define the orbit of a satellite. Notes: In amateur satellite operation, Keplerian elements mathematically describe a satellite's orbit so tracking software can predict its position over time. These orbital parameters are essential for knowing when and where to point antennas and when communication windows will occur.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A linear transponder can relay single sideband voice, FM, CW, RTTY, PSK, and other digital signals as long as they all fit within its passband. Notes: A linear transponder simply translates a range of input frequencies to a different output range, so any mode that occupies bandwidth within that passband can be relayed. This allows multiple analog and digital signal types to be used simultaneously on the same satellite transponder.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

Effective radiated power (ERP) should be limited to a satellite that uses a linear transponder to avoid reducing the downlink power to all other users. Notes: A linear transponder has a fixed total output power, so if one station transmits with excessive ERP, it forces the transponder to reduce power available to other signals. Limiting ERP helps ensure fair and reliable downlink signals for all users sharing the satellite transponder.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In amateur radio satellite and microwave operation, the terms “L band” and “S band” specify the 23- and 13-centimeter bands, respectively. Notes: On the Amateur Extra exam, “L band” and “S band” are defined by their corresponding amateur microwave allocations, which are the 23-centimeter and 13-centimeter bands. Knowing these band designations is important for understanding satellite and microwave operating practices and equipment.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A geostationary satellite appears to stay in one fixed position in the sky relative to an observer on Earth. Notes: A geostationary satellite orbits Earth at the equator with the same rotational period as Earth, so it appears motionless in the sky. This fixed apparent position makes it easy for amateur radio operators to point antennas and maintain continuous communication links.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A circularly polarized antenna can be used to minimize the effects of spin modulation and Faraday rotation. Notes: Circular polarization is less sensitive to changes in signal polarization caused by satellite spin and ionospheric Faraday rotation, so received signal strength varies less. This makes circularly polarized antennas especially useful for satellite and space communication links tested on the Amateur Extra exam.

Section E2B.

In digital television, a coding rate of 3/4 means that 25% of the data sent is forward error correction data. Notes: A coding rate of 3/4 indicates that three parts are payload data and one part is error correction, so one quarter (25%) of the transmitted bits are used for forward error correction. This balance improves signal reliability for digital TV while keeping most of the bandwidth available for program content.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

A fast-scan NTSC television frame is made up of 525 horizontal lines. Notes: In standard analog NTSC television, the video signal format is defined to contain 525 horizontal scan lines per frame. Amateur fast-scan TV uses this same NTSC standard, so each frame consists of 525 lines.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In a fast-scan NTSC television system, an interlaced scanning pattern is generated by scanning the odd-numbered lines in one field and the even-numbered lines in the next field. Notes: NTSC interlaced scanning divides each video frame into two fields, one containing all the odd lines and the other all the even lines. This approach effectively doubles the perceived refresh rate and reduces flicker in the displayed image, which is important for fast-scan amateur television signals.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In analog SSTV, color information is sent by transmitting the separate color lines sequentially. Notes: Analog SSTV encodes each line of the image one after another, including its color components, so the receiving station can reconstruct the full color picture line by line. This sequential transmission of color lines is fundamental to how analog SSTV images are built up over time in amateur radio.
[[SHORT_PAUSE]]

In analog fast-scan TV transmissions, vestigial sideband reduces the bandwidth while increasing the fidelity of low frequency video components. Notes: Vestigial sideband TV transmission partially suppresses one sideband to narrow the occupied spectrum while preserving enough of it to accurately reproduce low-frequency video information.
