Chapter E1: FCC Rules and Licensing.

Section E1A.

Transmitting a 3 kilohertz bandwidth upper side band signal with a carrier frequency of 14.348 megahertz is not legal because the upper 1 kilohertz of the signal extends beyond the upper edge of the 20-meter band.

When using a transceiver that displays the carrier frequency of phone signals, the lowest frequency at which a properly adjusted lower side band emission will be totally within the band is 3 kilohertz above the lower band edge.

The highest legal carrier frequency on the 20-meter band for transmitting a 2.8 kilohertz wide upper side band data signal is 14.1472 megahertz.

An Extra class operator may not answer the CQ of a station on 3.601 megahertz lower side band phone because the sideband components will extend beyond the edge of the phone band segment.

On any vessel or craft that is documented or registered in the United States, the station apparatus of an amateur station must be in the physical control of a person holding an FCC issued amateur license or who is authorized for alien reciprocal operation.

For channelized 60 meter operation, the CW signal must be transmitted at the center frequency of the channel.

The maximum power permitted on the 2200-meter band is 1 watt equivalent isotropic radiated power (EIRP).

If a station in a message forwarding system inadvertently forwards a message that is in violation of FCC rules, the control operator of the originating station is primarily accountable for the rules violation.

Except in some parts of Alaska, the maximum power permitted on the 630-meter band is 5 watts EIRP.

If an amateur station is installed aboard a ship or aircraft, its operation must be approved by the master of the ship or the pilot in command of the aircraft.

When operating an amateur station aboard a US-registered vessel in international waters, any FCC-issued amateur license is required.

Section E1B.

A spurious emission is an emission outside the signal’s necessary bandwidth that can be reduced or eliminated without affecting the information transmitted.

An acceptable bandwidth for digital voice or slow-scan TV transmissions made on the HF amateur bands is 3 kilohertz.

An amateur station must protect an FCC monitoring facility from harmful interference within a distance of 1 mile.

The control operator of a repeater operating in the 70-centimeter band must cease operation or make changes to the repeater that mitigate interference to a radiolocation system.

The National Radio Quiet Zone is an area surrounding the National Radio Astronomy Observatory.

If you are erecting an amateur station antenna structure at a site at or near a public use airport, you may have to notify the Federal Aviation Administration and register it with the FCC as required by Part 17 of the FCC rules.

PRB-1 applies to state and local zoning regulations.

If an amateur station’s signal causes interference to domestic broadcast reception and the receivers are of good engineering design, the FCC may require the amateur station to avoid transmitting during certain hours on frequencies that cause the interference.

Under RACES rules, any FCC-licensed amateur station that is certified by the responsible civil defense organization for the area served may be operated.

Under RACES rules, an amateur station may use all amateur service frequencies that are authorized to the control operator.

PRB-1 requires that state and local regulations affecting amateur radio antenna size and structures provide reasonable accommodations for amateur radio.

Section E1C.

The maximum bandwidth for a data emission on 60 meters is 2.8 kilohertz.

Communications transmitted to amateur stations in foreign countries must be limited to those incidental to the purpose of the amateur service and remarks of a personal nature.

An operator may begin operating on the 2200-meter or 630-meter band after 30 days of filing a notification with the Utilities Technology Council (UTC), provided they have not been informed that their station is within 1 kilometer of PLC systems using those frequencies.

An IARP is a permit that allows US amateurs to operate in certain countries of the Americas.

A station that is being automatically controlled may transmit third party communications only when transmitting RTTY or data emissions.

To operate in accordance with CEPT rules in foreign countries where permitted, you must have a copy of FCC Public Notice DA 16-1048.

Before transmitting on the 630- or 2200-meter bands, operators must inform the Utilities Technology Council (UTC) of their call sign and the coordinates of the station.

If the control link of a remotely controlled station malfunctions, the maximum permissible duration of the station’s transmissions is 3 minutes.

The highest modulation index permitted at the highest modulation frequency for angle modulation below 29.0 megahertz is 1.0.

The maximum mean power level for a spurious emission below 30 megahertz with respect to the fundamental emission is -43 dB.

CEPT operating arrangements allow an FCC-licensed US citizen to operate in many European countries, and amateurs from many European countries to operate in the US.

Phone emissions are permitted in the entire 630-meter band.

Section E1D.

Telemetry is the one-way transmission of measurements at a distance from the measuring instrument.

Telecommand signals from a space telecommand station may transmit encrypted messages.

A space telecommand station is an amateur station that transmits communications to initiate, modify, or terminate functions of a space station.

The identification transmissions from a balloon-borne telemetry station are required to include the station call sign.

When a station is being operated by telecommand on or within 50 kilometers of the Earth’s surface, all required identifying and control information specified by the rules must be posted at the station location.

The maximum permitted transmitter output power when operating a model craft by telecommand is 1 watt.

The HF amateur bands that include allocations for space stations are 40 meters, 20 meters, 15 meters, and 10 meters.

The 2 meter VHF amateur band has frequencies authorized for space stations.

The UHF amateur bands that have frequencies authorized for space stations are 70 centimeters and 13 centimeters.

Any amateur station so designated by the space station licensee is eligible to be a telecommand station of a space station, subject to the privileges of the class of operator license held by the control operator of the station.

Any amateur station may operate as an Earth station, subject to the privileges of the class of operator license held by the control operator.

One-way communications may be transmitted by a space station, a beacon station, or a telecommand station in the amateur service.

Section E1E.

Part 97 rules state that VEs and VECs may be reimbursed for out-of-pocket expenses incurred in preparing, processing, administering, and coordinating an examination for an amateur radio operator license.

Under Part 97, the VECs are responsible for maintaining the pools of questions for all US amateur license examinations.

A Volunteer Examiner Coordinator is an organization that has entered into an agreement with the FCC to coordinate, prepare, and administer amateur operator license examinations.

To be accredited as a Volunteer Examiner, a Volunteer Examiner Coordinator must confirm that the VE applicant meets all FCC requirements to serve as an examiner.

If the examinee does not pass the exam, the VE team must return the application document to the examinee.

Each administering VE is responsible for the proper conduct and necessary supervision during an amateur operator license examination session.

If a candidate fails to comply with the examiner’s instructions during an amateur operator license examination, the VE must immediately terminate the candidate’s examination.

A Volunteer Examiner may not administer an examination to relatives of the VE as listed in the FCC rules.

The penalty for a VE who fraudulently administers or certifies an examination may be revocation of the VE’s amateur station license grant and the suspension of the VE’s amateur operator license grant.

After administering a successful amateur operator license examination, the administering VEs must submit the application document to the coordinating VEC according to the coordinating VEC instructions.

If an examinee scores a passing grade on all examination elements needed for an upgrade or new license, three VEs must certify that the examinee is qualified for the license grant and that they have complied with the administering VE requirements.

Section E1F.

Spread spectrum transmissions are permitted only on amateur frequencies above 222 megahertz.

In the US, a person holding an amateur service license granted by the government of Canada is authorized the operating terms and conditions of the Canadian amateur service license, not to exceed US Amateur Extra class license privileges.

A dealer may sell an external RF power amplifier capable of operation below 144 megahertz if it has not been granted FCC certification when the amplifier is constructed or modified by an amateur radio operator for use at an amateur station.

Line A is a line roughly parallel to and south of the border between the US and Canada.

Amateur stations located in the contiguous 48 states and north of Line A may not transmit in the 420 megahertz - 430 megahertz frequency segment.

The FCC might issue a Special Temporary Authority (STA) to an amateur station to provide for experimental amateur communications.

An amateur station may send a message to a business when neither the amateur nor their employer has a pecuniary interest in the communications.

Amateur station communications transmitted for hire or material compensation are prohibited, except as otherwise provided in the rules.

Messages encoded to obscure their meaning cannot be transmitted over an amateur radio mesh network.

Only Technician, General, Advanced, or Amateur Extra class operators may be the control operator of an auxiliary station.

An external RF power amplifier must satisfy the FCC’s spurious emission standards when operated at the lesser of 1500 watts or its full output power to qualify for a grant of FCC certification.

Chapter E2: Operating and On-Air Procedures.

Section E2A.

The direction of an ascending pass for an amateur satellite is from south to north.

An inverting linear transponder reverses the sideband orientation of the input signal, outputs a signal with frequencies that decrease as the input frequencies increase, and can create a signal with the opposite modulation sense when a single-sideband signal is input.

In an inverting linear transponder, the upload signal is mixed with a local oscillator signal and the difference product is transmitted.

The mode of an amateur radio satellite is the satellite’s uplink and downlink frequency bands.

The letters in a satellite’s mode designator specify the uplink and downlink frequency ranges.

Keplerian elements are parameters that define the orbit of a satellite.

All of the listed types of signals can be relayed through a linear transponder.

Effective radiated power (ERP) should be limited to a satellite that uses a linear transponder to avoid reducing the downlink power to all other users.

The terms “L band” and “S band” specify the 23- and 13-centimeter bands.

A geostationary satellite appears to stay in one position in the sky.

A circularly polarized antenna can be used to minimize the effects of spin modulation and Faraday rotation.

Section E2B.

In digital television, a coding rate of 3/4 means that 25% of the data sent is forward error correction data.

A fast-scan (NTSC) television frame is made up of 525 horizontal lines.

In a fast-scan (NTSC) television system, an interlaced scanning pattern is generated by scanning odd-numbered lines in one field and even-numbered lines in the next.

In analog SSTV, color information is sent by transmitting the color lines sequentially.

In analog fast-scan TV transmissions, vestigial sideband reduces the bandwidth while increasing the fidelity of low frequency video components.

Vestigial sideband modulation is amplitude modulation in which one complete sideband and a portion of the other are transmitted.

Amateur television DVB-T signals use QAM and QPSK modulation types.

Commercial analog TV receivers can be used for fast-scan TV operations on the 70-centimeter band by transmitting on channels shared with cable TV.

An single side band receiver can be used to receive and decode SSTV using the Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) protocol.

In an analog slow-scan television signal, the brightness of the picture is encoded by the tone frequency.

The function of the vertical interval signaling (VIS) code sent as part of an SSTV transmission is to identify the SSTV mode being used.

Slow-scan television (SSTV) receiving software is signaled to begin a new picture line by specific tone frequencies.

Section E2C.

When a US-licensed operator is operating a station via remote control and the remote transmitter is located in the US, no additional indicator is required.

The ADIF file format is used for exchanging amateur radio log data.

Amateur radio contesting is generally excluded from the 30 meter band.

Amateur radio mesh networks can use frequencies that are shared with various unlicensed wireless data services.

A DX QSL Manager handles the receiving and sending of confirmations for a DX station.

During a VHF/UHF contest, the highest level of single side band or CW activity is typically found in the weak signal segment of the band, with most of the activity near the calling frequency.

The Cabrillo format is a standard for submission of electronic contest logs.

Contacts in Logbook of The World (LoTW) may be confirmed for contacts using digital modes, contacts using phone modes, and contacts using CW modes.

An amateur radio mesh network is commonly implemented using a wireless router running custom firmware.

DX stations often transmit and receive on different frequencies in order to reduce interference, improve operating efficiency, and better manage congestion on the band.

When attempting to contact a DX station during a contest or in a pileup, you should generally identify your station by sending your full call sign once or twice.

The delay between a control operator action and the corresponding change in the transmitted signal is called latency.

Section E2D.

MSK144 is a digital mode designed for meteor scatter communications.

In a VHF contest using the FT8 or FT4 modes, the signal-to-noise ratio information is replaced by the grid square.

Q65 is a digital mode specifically designed for EME communications.

Real-time tracking of balloons carrying amateur radio transmitters uses APRS.

JT65 mode decodes signals with a very low signal-to-noise ratio.

A method for establishing EME contacts is to use time-synchronous transmissions that alternate between stations.

APRS uses the AX.25 digital protocol.

APRS beacon data is transmitted using an Unnumbered Information packet frame.

JT65 uses multitone AFSK modulation.

The packet path WIDE3-1 designates that three digipeater hops are requested with one hop remaining.

APRS stations relay data by packet digipeaters.

Section E2E.

FSK modulation is used for data emissions below 30 megahertz.

WSJT-X digital mode transmit and receive timing is synchronized by synchronization of computer clocks.

The "4" in FT4 refers to four-tone continuous-phase frequency shift keying.

The FST4 mode is characterized by very narrow bandwidth, long transmission cycles, and highly sensitive reception primarily intended for LF and MF bands.

WSPR is a digital mode that does not support keyboard-to-keyboard operation.

An FT8 transmission cycle has a length of 15 seconds.

Q65 differs from JT65 in that multiple receive cycles are averaged.

PACTOR is an HF digital mode that can be used to transfer binary files.

PSK31 is an HF digital mode that uses variable-length character coding.

FT8 is the digital mode that has the narrowest bandwidth.

In direct FSK, the transmitted signal is frequency shift keyed by directly modulating the transmitter VFO.

ALE stations establish contact by constantly scanning a list of frequencies and activating the radio when the designated call sign is received.

PACTOR IV has the highest data throughput under clear communication conditions among the listed digital modes.

Chapter E3: Radio Wave Propagation Basics.

Section E3A.

The approximate maximum separation measured along the surface of the Earth between two stations communicating by EME is 12,000 miles if the moon is visible by both stations.

Libration fading of an EME signal is characterized by a fluttery, irregular fading.

When scheduling EME contacts, the least path loss generally occurs when the Moon is at perigee.

An electromagnetic wave travels at a right angle to both the electric field and the magnetic field.

The component fields of an electromagnetic wave are oriented at right angles.

To continue a long-distance contact when the MUF for that path decreases due to darkness, you should switch to a lower frequency HF band.

Atmospheric ducts capable of propagating microwave signals often form over large bodies of water.

When a meteor strikes the Earth’s atmosphere, it forms a linear ionized region in the E region of the ionosphere.

Meteor-scatter communications are most effective using frequencies in the range of 28 megahertz to 148 megahertz.

The speed of electromagnetic waves through a medium is determined by the index of refraction.

A typical range for tropospheric duct propagation of microwave signals is 100 miles to 300 miles.

Auroral propagation is most likely to result from severe geomagnetic storms.

CW is the emission mode that is best for auroral propagation.

Circularly polarized electromagnetic waves are waves with rotating electric and magnetic fields.

Section E3B.

Transequatorial propagation (TEP) is most likely to occur between points separated by 2,000 miles to 3,000 miles over a path perpendicular to the geomagnetic equator.

The approximate maximum range for signals using transequatorial propagation is 5,000 miles.

Transequatorial propagation is most likely to occur during the afternoon or early evening.

“Extraordinary” and “ordinary” waves are independently propagating, elliptically polarized waves created in the ionosphere.

A path entirely in darkness is most likely to support long-distance propagation on 160 meters.

Long-path propagation is most frequent on the 40 meters and 20 meters amateur bands.

Lowering a signal’s transmitted elevation angle increases the distance covered by each ionospheric HF skip propagation hop.

The maximum range of ground-wave propagation decreases when the signal frequency is increased.

Sporadic-E propagation is most likely to occur around the solstices, especially the summer solstice.

In chordal-hop propagation, the signal experiences less loss than in multi-hop propagation that uses the Earth as a reflector.

Sporadic-E propagation is most likely to occur between sunrise and sunset.

Chordal-hop propagation is successive ionospheric refractions without an intermediate reflection from the ground.

Ground-wave propagation supports vertical polarization.

Section E3C.

Short-term radio blackouts are caused by solar flares.

A rising A-index or K-index indicates increasing disturbance of the geomagnetic field.

Signal paths that pass through the auroral oval are most likely to experience high levels of absorption when the A-index or K-index is elevated.

The value of Bz (B sub z) represents the north-south strength of the interplanetary magnetic field.

A southward orientation of Bz (B sub z) increases the likelihood that charged particles from the Sun will cause disturbed conditions.

The VHF/UHF radio horizon is approximately 15 percent farther than the geographic horizon.

Class X indicates the greatest solar flare intensity.

The space-weather term for an extreme geomagnetic storm is G5.

Amateur radio propagation reporting networks report data about digital-mode and CW signals.

The 304A solar parameter measures UV emissions at 304 angstroms that are correlated to the solar flux index.

VOACAP software models HF propagation.

A sudden rise in radio background noise across a large portion of the HF spectrum indicates that a coronal mass ejection impact or a solar flare has occurred.

Chapter E4: Amateur Station Operating Practices.

Section E4A.

The sampling rate of the analog-to-digital converter limits the highest frequency signal that can be accurately displayed on a digital oscilloscope.

A spectrum analyzer displays signal amplitude on the vertical axis and frequency on the horizontal axis.

A spectrum analyzer is used to display spurious signals and intermodulation distortion products generated by an single side band transmitter.

Compensation of an oscilloscope probe is performed by displaying a square wave and adjusting the probe until the horizontal portions of the displayed wave are as nearly flat as possible.

A prescaler is used with a frequency counter to reduce the signal frequency to within the counter's operating range.

On a digital oscilloscope, aliasing causes a false, jittery low-frequency version of the waveform to be displayed.

An advantage of using an antenna analyzer compared to an SWR bridge is that antenna analyzers compute SWR and impedance automatically.

All of the listed instruments can be used to measure SWR.

When using an oscilloscope probe, it is good practice to minimize the length of the probe's ground connection.

When using an oscilloscope to measure a linear power supply’s output ripple, the most effective trigger mode is Line.

An antenna analyzer can measure antenna system impedances, coaxial cable electrical length, and antenna radiation resistance.

Section E4B.

The factor that most affects the accuracy of a frequency counter is its time base accuracy.

For a voltmeter, multiplying its full-scale reading by its sensitivity in ohms per volt gives the input impedance of the voltmeter.

The S parameter equivalent to forward gain is S21.

The S parameter that represents input port return loss or reflection coefficient (equivalent to VSWR) is S11.

An RF vector network analyzer is calibrated using three test loads: a short circuit, an open circuit, and a 50 ohms load.

When a directional power meter connected between a transmitter and a terminating load reads 100 watts forward power and 25 watts reflected power, the power being absorbed by the load is 75 watts.

The subscripts of S parameters represent the port or ports at which measurements are made.

The Q of a series-tuned circuit can be determined from the bandwidth of the circuit's frequency response.

A two-port vector network analyzer can be used to measure filter frequency response.

Intermodulation distortion in an single side band transmitter is measured by modulating the transmitter using two AF signals having non-harmonically related frequencies and observing the RF output with a spectrum analyzer.

A vector network analyzer can measure antenna impedance, complex impedance, and standing wave ratio (SWR).

Section E4C.

Excessive phase noise in an SDR receiver’s master clock oscillator can combine with strong signals on nearby frequencies to generate interference.

A front-end filter or preselector can be effective in eliminating interference from strong out-of-band signals.

The suppression in an FM receiver of one signal by another stronger signal on the same frequency is called the capture effect.

The noise figure of a receiver is the ratio in dB of the noise generated by the receiver to the theoretical minimum noise.

A receiver noise floor of -174 dBm represents the theoretical noise in a 1 Hz bandwidth at the input of a perfect receiver at room temperature.

Increasing a receiver’s bandwidth from 50 Hz to 1,000 Hz increases the receiver’s noise floor by 13 dB.

The MDS of a receiver represents the minimum discernible signal.

An SDR receiver is overloaded when input signals exceed the reference voltage of the analog-to-digital converter.

A good reason for selecting a high IF for a superheterodyne HF or VHF communications receiver is that it is easier for the front-end circuitry to eliminate image responses.

An advantage of having a variety of receiver bandwidths from which to select is that the receive bandwidth can be set to match the modulation bandwidth, maximizing signal-to-noise ratio and minimizing interference.

Input attenuation reduces receiver overload on the lower frequency HF bands with little or no impact on signal-to-noise ratio because atmospheric noise is generally greater than internally generated noise even after attenuation.

A narrow-band roofing filter improves blocking dynamic range by attenuating strong signals near the receive frequency.

Reciprocal mixing is local oscillator phase noise mixing with adjacent strong signals to create interference to desired signals.

The purpose of the receiver IF Shift control is to reduce interference from stations transmitting on adjacent frequencies.

Section E4D.

The blocking dynamic range of a receiver is the difference in dB between the noise floor and the level of an incoming signal that will cause 1 dB of gain compression.

Poor dynamic range in a receiver causes spurious signals due to cross modulation and desensitization from strong adjacent signals.

Intermodulation interference between two repeaters in close proximity is created when the output signals mix in the final amplifier of one or both transmitters.

Desensitization is the reduction in receiver sensitivity caused by a strong signal near the received frequency.

Receiver desensitization is made less likely by inserting attenuation before the first RF stage.

Intermodulation in an electronic circuit is caused by nonlinear circuits or devices.

The purpose of the preselector in a communications receiver is to increase the rejection of signals outside the band being received.

A third-order intercept level of 40 dBm means that a pair of 40 dBm input signals will theoretically generate a third-order intermodulation product that has the same output amplitude as either of the input signals.

Odd-order intermodulation products created by two signals in the band being received are of particular interest because they are also likely to fall within the same band.

The link margin in a system with a transmit power level of 10 W (+40 dBm), a system antenna gain of 10 dBi, a cable loss of 3 dB, a path loss of 136 dB, a receiver minimum discernable signal of -103 dBm, and a required signal-to-noise ratio of 6 dB is +8 dB.

The received signal level with a transmit power of 10 W (+40 dBm), a transmit antenna gain of 6 dBi, a receive antenna gain of 3 dBi, and a path loss of 100 dB is -51 dBm.

A receiver minimum discernible signal of -100 dBm represents a power level of 0.1 picowatts.

Section E4E.

When using an automatic notch filter to remove interfering carriers while receiving CW signals, the filter can remove the CW signal as well as the interfering carrier.

Digital noise reduction can often reduce atmospheric, impulse, power line, and line noise.

Conducted noise from an automobile battery charging system can be suppressed by installing ferrite chokes on the charging system leads.

Radio frequency interference from a line-driven AC motor is suppressed by using a brute-force AC-line filter in series with the motor’s power leads.

Computer network equipment can cause electrical interference characterized by the appearance of unstable modulated or unmodulated signals at specific frequencies.

Shielded cables can radiate or receive interference when common-mode currents flow on the shield and conductors.

Common-mode current flows equally on all conductors of an unshielded multiconductor cable.

When using a noise blanker, strong signals may be distorted and appear to cause spurious emissions.

Intermittent loud roaring or buzzing AC line interference can be created by a faulty power line noise suppressor, arcing contacts in a thermostatically controlled device, or a defective doorbell or doorbell transformer.

Local AM broadcast band signals can combine and generate spurious signals on the MF or HF bands when nearby corroded metal connections mix and reradiate the broadcast signals.

Interference received as a series of carriers at regular intervals across a wide frequency range is caused by switch-mode power supplies.

A station AC surge protector should be installed on the single point ground panel.

The purpose of a single point ground panel is to ensure all lightning protectors activate at the same time.

Chapter E5: Core Electrical Principles.

Section E5A.

Resonance can cause the voltage across reactances in a series RLC circuit to be higher than the voltage applied to the entire circuit.

The resonant frequency of an RLC circuit with R of 22 ohms, L of 50 microhenries, and C of 40 picofarads is 3.56 megahertz.

At resonance, the magnitude of the impedance of a series RLC circuit is approximately equal to the circuit resistance.

At resonance, the magnitude of the impedance of a parallel RLC circuit is approximately equal to the circuit resistance.

Increasing the Q of an impedance-matching circuit decreases the matching bandwidth.

The magnitude of the circulating current within the components of a parallel LC circuit at resonance is at a maximum.

The magnitude of the current at the input of a parallel RLC circuit at resonance is minimum.

At resonance in a series resonant circuit, the voltage and current are in phase.

The Q of an RLC parallel resonant circuit is calculated as the resistance divided by the reactance of either the inductance or the capacitance.

The resonant frequency of an RLC circuit with R equal to 33 ohms, L equal to 50 microhenries, and C equal to 10 picofarads is 7.12 megahertz.

A resonant circuit with a resonant frequency of 7.1 megahertz and a Q of 150 has a half-power bandwidth of 47.3 kilohertz.

A resonant circuit with a resonant frequency of 3.7 megahertz and a Q of 118 has a half-power bandwidth of 31.4 kilohertz.

In a series resonant circuit, increasing the Q causes the internal voltages to increase.

Section E5B.

When pure reactance is converted to susceptance, its magnitude is replaced by its reciprocal.

Susceptance is the imaginary part of admittance.

In a series RLC circuit where XC is 500 ohms, R is 1 kilohm, and XL is 250 ohms, the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through the circuit is 14.0 degrees with the voltage lagging the current.

In a series RLC circuit where XC is 300 ohms, R is 100 ohms, and XL is 100 ohms, the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through the circuit is 63 degrees with the voltage lagging the current.

In an AC circuit, the current through a capacitor leads the voltage across the capacitor by 90 degrees.

In an inductor with AC applied, the voltage across the inductor leads the current through the inductor by 90 degrees.

In a series RLC circuit where XC is 25 ohms, R is 100 ohms, and XL is 75 ohms, the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through the circuit is 27 degrees with the voltage leading the current.

Admittance is the inverse of impedance.
