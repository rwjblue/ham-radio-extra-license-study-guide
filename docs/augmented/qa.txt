Notes: USB voice signals extend from the carrier frequency upward by their bandwidth, so a 3 kHz USB signal at 14.348 MHz occupies 14.348 to 14.351 MHz. This causes the top 1 kHz of the signal to fall outside the authorized 20-meter band, making the transmission illegal.

Notes: An LSB phone signal occupies bandwidth extending above the displayed carrier frequency, so the carrier must be set high enough to keep the entire sideband inside the band. A 3 kHz offset above the lower band edge ensures that a typical 3 kHz-wide LSB signal remains completely within the authorized frequency range.
E1A03: Q: What is the highest legal carrier frequency on the 20-meter band for transmitting a 2.8 kHz wide USB data signal? A: 14.1472 MHz.
Notes: The upper band edge for 20 meters is 14.150 MHz, and FCC rules require that the entire transmitted signal, including its 2.8 kHz bandwidth, remain within the band. Subtracting 2.8 kHz from 14.150 MHz results in a maximum carrier frequency of 14.1472 MHz.
E1A04: Q: May an Extra class operator answer the CQ of a station on 3.601 MHz LSB phone? A: No, the sideband components will extend beyond the edge of the phone band segment.
Notes: In LSB phone operation, the occupied bandwidth extends downward from the suppressed carrier frequency, so transmitting on 3.601 MHz would cause part of the signal to fall outside the authorized phone subband. FCC rules require that all transmitted energy remain within the designated phone band segment.
E1A05: Q: Who must be in physical control of the station apparatus of an amateur station aboard any vessel or craft that is documented or registered in the United States? A: Any person holding an FCC issued amateur license or who is authorized for alien reciprocal operation.
Notes: FCC rules require that amateur stations on U.S.-documented or registered vessels be controlled by someone who is legally authorized to operate under U.S. amateur service regulations. This includes licensed U.S. amateurs and foreign amateurs operating under valid reciprocal operating authority.
E1A06: Q: What is the required transmit frequency of a CW signal for channelized 60 meter operation? A: At the center frequency of the channel.
Notes: On the 60 meter band, FCC rules require all emissions, including CW, to be centered in the assigned channel to avoid interference and maintain the channelized structure. Tuning to the channel center frequency ensures compliance with the band’s specific channel-based allocation.
E1A07: Q: What is the maximum power permitted on the 2200-meter band? A: 1 watt EIRP (equivalent isotropic radiated power).
Notes: FCC rules limit amateur operation on the 2200-meter band to 1 watt EIRP to minimize potential interference to other services using this low-frequency spectrum. EIRP includes both transmitter power and antenna gain, so amateurs must ensure the total radiated power does not exceed this 1 watt limit.
E1A08: Q: If a station in a message forwarding system inadvertently forwards a message that is in violation of FCC rules, who is primarily accountable for the rules violation? A: The control operator of the originating station.

E1A09: Q: Except in some parts of Alaska, what is the maximum power permitted on the 630-meter band? A: 5 watts EIRP (equivalent isotropic radiated power).

EIRP accounts for both transmitter output power and antenna gain, so operators must calculate their system to stay within this 5-watt EIRP limit.
E1A10: Q: If an amateur station is installed aboard a ship or aircraft, what condition must be met before the station is operated? A: Its operation must be approved by the master of the ship or the pilot in command of the aircraft.
Notes: The FCC requires that the person in overall command of the vessel or aircraft authorize amateur station operation to ensure safety and operational control. This keeps radio use coordinated with the ship’s or aircraft’s primary communications and safety responsibilities.
E1A11: Q: What licensing is required when operating an amateur station aboard a US-registered vessel in international waters? A: Any FCC-issued amateur license.
Notes: For a US-registered vessel, FCC amateur service rules apply regardless of location, including international waters. Therefore, any valid FCC-issued amateur license grants the operator permission to use an amateur station on board.

### Group E1B - Station locations, restrictions, and special operations
E1B01: Q: Which of the following constitutes a spurious emission? A: An emission outside the signal’s necessary bandwidth that can be reduced or eliminated without affecting the information transmitted.
Notes: In amateur radio, any signal energy that lies outside the bandwidth needed to carry the intended information and can be removed without degrading that information is considered spurious. Spurious emissions are unwanted and must be limited to comply with FCC technical standards and prevent interference to other users.
E1B02: Q: Which of the following is an acceptable bandwidth for digital voice or slow-scan TV transmissions made on the HF amateur bands? A: 3 kHz.
Notes: On the HF amateur bands, digital voice and slow-scan TV signals are expected to fit within a typical SSB channel, which is about 3 kHz wide. This bandwidth limit helps prevent interference and promotes efficient spectrum use as required by FCC rules for HF operation.
E1B03: Q: Within what distance must an amateur station protect an FCC monitoring facility from harmful interference? A: 1 mile.
Notes: FCC monitoring stations perform sensitive signal measurements, so nearby amateur stations must avoid causing harmful interference. The 1 mile distance defines the protected zone around these facilities in the Amateur Extra rules.
E1B04: Q: What must the control operator of a repeater operating in the 70-centimeter band do if a radiolocation system experiences interference from that repeater? A: Cease operation or make changes to the repeater that mitigate the interference.
Notes: In the 70-centimeter band, amateur repeaters are secondary users and must not cause harmful interference to primary services such as radiolocation. Therefore, the control operator is required to stop operation or modify the repeater so that the interference is resolved.
E1B05: Q: What is the National Radio Quiet Zone? A: An area surrounding the National Radio Astronomy Observatory.
Notes: Radio emissions are restricted in the National Radio Quiet Zone to protect the sensitive observations made by the National Radio Astronomy Observatory. This helps minimize radio frequency interference to important radio astronomy research facilities located there.
E1B06: Q: Which of the following additional rules apply if you are erecting an amateur station antenna structure at a site at or near a public use airport? A: You may have to notify the Federal Aviation Administration and register it with the FCC as required by Part 17 of the FCC rules.
Notes: Antenna structures near public use airports can pose potential hazards to air navigation, so the FAA and FCC require notice and registration under Part 17. This ensures the structure is properly evaluated, marked, and recorded for aviation safety and regulatory compliance.
E1B07: Q: To what type of regulations does PRB-1 apply? A: State and local zoning.
Notes: PRB-1 is an FCC policy requiring state and local governments to reasonably accommodate amateur radio antennas in their zoning and land-use regulations. It specifically addresses how these non-federal authorities regulate the placement and height of amateur radio antenna structures.
E1B08: Q: What limitations may the FCC place on an amateur station if its signal causes interference to domestic broadcast reception, assuming that the receivers involved are of good engineering design? A: The amateur station must avoid transmitting during certain hours on frequencies that cause the interference.
Notes: The FCC can limit when an amateur station may operate on particular frequencies to prevent harmful interference to properly designed broadcast receivers. This protects domestic broadcast reception while still allowing amateur operation at other times or on other frequencies.
E1B09: Q: Which amateur stations may be operated under RACES rules? A: Any FCC-licensed amateur station certified by the responsible civil defense organization for the area served.
Notes: RACES operation is restricted to stations that are both FCC-licensed and specifically certified by the local civil defense authority. This ensures that emergency communications are coordinated and authorized within the served area during emergencies and civil defense activities.
E1B10: Q: What frequencies are authorized to an amateur station operating under RACES rules? A: All amateur service frequencies authorized to the control operator.
Notes: RACES operation does not grant additional frequency privileges, so the station is limited to the same amateur bands and segments the control operator is already authorized to use. This ensures emergency communications stay within the operator’s existing license class privileges and FCC regulations.
E1B11: Q: What does PRB-1 require of state and local regulations affecting amateur radio antenna size and structures? A: Reasonable accommodations of amateur radio must be made.
Notes: Under PRB-1, the FCC directs state and local governments to reasonably accommodate amateur radio operations when regulating antennas. This protects the ability of licensed amateurs to communicate while still allowing local authorities to enforce legitimate zoning and safety regulations.

This maintains the noncommercial, self-training, and hobby nature of amateur radio across borders.

This coordination step is part of the FCC rules specific to operation on the 630- and 2200-meter bands.
E1C08: Q: What is the maximum permissible duration of a remotely controlled station’s transmissions if its control link malfunctions? A: 3 minutes.
Notes: FCC rules limit a remotely controlled station to no more than 3 minutes of transmitting after control is lost to prevent unchecked, potentially interfering signals. This time limit helps ensure that malfunctioning or uncontrolled transmissions do not continue indefinitely on the air.
E1C09: Q: What is the highest modulation index permitted at the highest modulation frequency for angle modulation below 29.0 MHz? A: 1.0.
Notes: FCC rules limit the modulation index for angle modulation below 29.0 MHz to 1.0 at the highest modulation frequency to control occupied bandwidth and interference. This ensures the signal remains within its authorized emission limits for Amateur Extra operators.
E1C10: Q: What is the maximum mean power level for a spurious emission below 30 MHz with respect to the fundamental emission? A: - 43 dB.
Notes: FCC rules limit spurious emissions to -43 dB below the carrier to prevent interference on adjacent and nearby frequencies. This ensures that unwanted emissions are much weaker than the intended signal while operating below 30 MHz.
E1C11: Q: Which of the following operating arrangements allows an FCC-licensed US citizen to operate in many European countries, and amateurs from many European countries to operate in the US? A: CEPT.
Notes: CEPT is an international reciprocal operating agreement that standardizes temporary amateur radio operating privileges among member countries. Under CEPT, eligible US and European amateurs can legally operate in each other’s countries without obtaining an additional local amateur license.
E1C12: Q: In what portion of the 630-meter band are phone emissions permitted? A: The entire band.

E1D01: Q: What is the definition of telemetry? A: One-way transmission of measurements at a distance from the measuring instrument.
Notes: In the Amateur Extra exam context, telemetry refers specifically to sending measurement or status data in one direction from the remote instrument back to a control or monitoring point. This definition distinguishes it from two-way control or voice communications.
E1D02: Q: Which of the following may transmit encrypted messages? A: Telecommand signals from a space telecommand station.
Notes: The FCC rules specifically allow encryption when it is used for telecommand of a space station or radio control from a space telecommand station. This exception permits secure control of satellites while maintaining compliance with amateur service regulations.
E1D03: Q: What is a space telecommand station? A: An amateur station that transmits communications to initiate, modify, or terminate functions of a space station.
Notes: In the Amateur Extra exam context, a space telecommand station specifically controls the operation of an amateur space station by sending signals that start, change, or stop its functions. This definition aligns with FCC rules governing amateur operation of satellites and other space-based stations.
E1D04: Q: Which of the following is required in the identification transmissions from a balloon-borne telemetry station? A: Call sign.
Notes: The FCC requires all amateur stations, including balloon-borne telemetry stations, to identify with their call sign so the transmitting station can be clearly and uniquely identified. Including the call sign in the telemetry identification satisfies this regulatory requirement for proper station identification on the Amateur Extra exam.

E1D06: Q: What is the maximum permitted transmitter output power when operating a model craft by telecommand? A: 1 watt.

E1D08: Q: Which VHF amateur bands have frequencies authorized for space stations? A: 2 meters.

E1D09: Q: Which UHF amateur bands have frequencies authorized for space stations? A: 70 centimeters and 13 centimeters.

E1D12: Q: Which of the following amateur stations may transmit one-way communications? A: A space station, beacon station, or telecommand station.

## SUBELEMENT E2 - Operating and On-Air Procedures
### Group E2A - Amateur satellites, orbits, and space operation
E2A01: Q: What is the direction of an ascending pass for an amateur satellite? A: From south to north.
Notes: An ascending pass is defined as the portion of the satellite’s orbit where it moves from the Southern Hemisphere toward the Northern Hemisphere as seen from Earth. For an observer, this corresponds to the satellite traveling generally from south to north across the sky.
E2A02: Q: Which of the following is characteristic of an inverting linear transponder? A: All these choices are correct:

  - Doppler shift is reduced because the uplink and downlink shifts are in opposite directions
  - Signal position in the band is reversed
  - Upper sideband on the uplink becomes lower sideband on the downlink, and vice versa
Notes: Each listed behavior describes how an inverting linear transponder processes signals in amateur satellite operation. Because it inverts both sideband sense and passband frequency while maintaining linearity, all of the provided statements are simultaneously true.
E2A03: Q: How is an upload signal processed by an inverting linear transponder? A: The signal is mixed with a local oscillator signal and the difference product is transmitted.
Notes: An inverting linear transponder uses frequency mixing so that the uplink signal is converted to a different frequency by subtracting it from a local oscillator signal. This difference product is then amplified and retransmitted, preserving the modulation while shifting the frequency and inverting the spectrum as used in satellite and similar amateur applications.
E2A04: Q: What is meant by the “mode” of an amateur radio satellite? A: The satellite’s uplink and downlink frequency bands.
Notes: In amateur satellite operation, the mode label tells operators which frequency band is used for transmitting to the satellite (uplink) and which band is used for receiving from it (downlink). This lets operators choose appropriate equipment and frequencies for successful communication through the satellite.
E2A05: Q: What do the letters in a satellite’s mode designator specify? A: The uplink and downlink frequency ranges.
Notes: Each letter in the satellite mode (such as Mode U/V or L/S) corresponds to a specific amateur band used for the uplink and the downlink. This lets operators know which frequency ranges to use when communicating through that satellite.
E2A06: Q: What are Keplerian elements? A: Parameters that define the orbit of a satellite.
Notes: In amateur satellite operation, Keplerian elements mathematically describe a satellite's orbit so tracking software can predict its position over time. These orbital parameters are essential for knowing when and where to point antennas and when communication windows will occur.
E2A07: Q: Which of the following types of signals can be relayed through a linear transponder? A: All these choices are correct:

  - FM and CW
  - SSB and SSTV
  - PSK and packet
Notes: A linear transponder simply translates a range of input frequencies to a different output range, so any mode that occupies bandwidth within that passband can be relayed. This allows multiple analog and digital signal types to be used simultaneously on the same satellite transponder.
E2A08: Q: Why should effective radiated power (ERP) be limited to a satellite that uses a linear transponder? A: To avoid reducing the downlink power to all other users.
Notes: A linear transponder has a fixed total output power, so if one station transmits with excessive ERP, it forces the transponder to reduce power available to other signals. Limiting ERP helps ensure fair and reliable downlink signals for all users sharing the satellite transponder.
E2A09: Q: What do the terms “L band” and “S band” specify? A: The 23- and 13-centimeter bands.
Notes: On the Amateur Extra exam, “L band” and “S band” are defined by their corresponding amateur microwave allocations, which are the 23-centimeter and 13-centimeter bands. Knowing these band designations is important for understanding satellite and microwave operating practices and equipment.
E2A10: Q: What type of satellite appears to stay in one position in the sky? A: Geostationary.
Notes: A geostationary satellite orbits Earth at the equator with the same rotational period as Earth, so it appears motionless in the sky. This fixed apparent position makes it easy for amateur radio operators to point antennas and maintain continuous communication links.
E2A11: Q: What type of antenna can be used to minimize the effects of spin modulation and Faraday rotation? A: A circularly polarized antenna.
Notes: Circular polarization is less sensitive to changes in signal polarization caused by satellite spin and ionospheric Faraday rotation, so received signal strength varies less. This makes circularly polarized antennas especially useful for satellite and space communication links tested on the Amateur Extra exam.

### Group E2B - Fast-scan and slow-scan TV techniques
E2B01: Q: In digital television, what does a coding rate of 3/4 mean? A: 25% of the data sent is forward error correction data.
Notes: A coding rate of 3/4 indicates that three parts are payload data and one part is error correction, so one quarter (25%) of the transmitted bits are used for forward error correction. This balance improves signal reliability for digital TV while keeping most of the bandwidth available for program content.
E2B02: Q: How many horizontal lines make up a fast-scan (NTSC) television frame? A: 525.
Notes: In standard analog NTSC television, the video signal format is defined to contain 525 horizontal scan lines per frame. Amateur fast-scan TV uses this same NTSC standard, so each frame consists of 525 lines.
E2B03: Q: How is an interlaced scanning pattern generated in a fast-scan (NTSC) television system? A: By scanning odd-numbered lines in one field and even-numbered lines in the next.
Notes: NTSC interlaced scanning divides each video frame into two fields, one containing all the odd lines and the other all the even lines. This approach effectively doubles the perceived refresh rate and reduces flicker in the displayed image, which is important for fast-scan amateur television signals.
E2B04: Q: How is color information sent in analog SSTV? A: Color lines are sent sequentially.

## SUBELEMENT E3 - Radio Wave Propagation Basics
### Group E3A - EME, meteor, tropospheric, and auroral propagation
E3A01: Q: What is the approximate maximum separation measured along the surface of the Earth between two stations communicating by EME? A: 12,000 miles, if the moon is “visible” by both stations.
Notes: Earth-Moon-Earth (EME) communication requires that both stations have a line of sight to the Moon, which limits their maximum separation along Earth's surface to about 12,000 miles. This distance corresponds to the largest arc on the globe where the Moon can be simultaneously above the horizon for both locations.
E3A02: Q: What characterizes libration fading of an EME signal? A: A fluttery, irregular fading.
Notes: On Earth-Moon-Earth (EME) paths, small changes in the reflecting surface of the Moon and in the propagation path cause rapid, uneven variations in signal strength. This produces the distinctive fluttery, irregular fading known as libration fading on EME signals.
E3A03: Q: When scheduling EME contacts, which of these conditions will generally result in the least path loss? A: When the Moon is at perigee.
Notes: Path loss is reduced when the distance between Earth and the Moon is minimized, which happens at perigee. The shorter path reduces free-space loss, improving signal strength for EME communication.
E3A04: Q: In what direction does an electromagnetic wave travel? A: It travels at a right angle to the electric and magnetic fields.
Notes: In free space, the electric and magnetic fields of an electromagnetic wave are mutually perpendicular and the wave propagates in the third, perpendicular direction. This right-angle relationship among the electric field, magnetic field, and direction of travel is fundamental to understanding radio wave propagation for the Amateur Extra exam.
E3A05: Q: How are the component fields of an electromagnetic wave oriented? A: They are at right angles.
Notes: In radio waves, the electric and magnetic components are mutually perpendicular, forming a transverse wave. This right-angle relationship is fundamental to how electromagnetic waves propagate in free space and is essential to understanding antenna polarization and radiation patterns.
E3A06: Q: What should be done to continue a long-distance contact when the MUF for that path decreases due to darkness? A: Switch to a lower frequency HF band.
Notes: As darkness lowers the Maximum Usable Frequency (MUF), higher HF bands may no longer support reliable ionospheric propagation on that path. Moving to a lower frequency HF band keeps the signal below the new MUF, allowing the contact to continue.
E3A07: Q: Atmospheric ducts capable of propagating microwave signals often form over what geographic feature? A: Large bodies of water.
Notes: Temperature inversions and stable air layers that create atmospheric ducts are commonly found over large bodies of water. These ducts can trap and guide microwave signals, allowing extended propagation ranges useful to amateur radio operators on microwave bands.
E3A08: Q: When a meteor strikes the Earth’s atmosphere, a linear ionized region is formed at what region of the ionosphere? A: The E region.
Notes: Meteor trails typically ionize the atmosphere at altitudes corresponding to the E region of the ionosphere. This temporary ionized path can reflect VHF radio signals, enabling brief long-distance communication useful in amateur radio meteor-scatter modes.
E3A09: Q: Which of the following frequency ranges is most suited for meteor-scatter communications? A: 28 MHz - 148 MHz.
Notes: Meteor trails in the ionosphere efficiently reflect radio signals in the VHF and upper HF range, making 28 MHz to 148 MHz particularly effective for meteor-scatter contacts. This range balances good reflection efficiency with manageable path loss and antenna practicality for amateur radio use.
E3A10: Q: What determines the speed of electromagnetic waves through a medium? A: The index of refraction.
Notes: In radio and wave propagation theory, the index of refraction describes how much a medium slows electromagnetic waves compared to their speed in a vacuum. A higher index of refraction means a lower wave speed in that medium, which directly affects propagation behavior for amateur radio signals.
E3A11: Q: What is a typical range for tropospheric duct propagation of microwave signals? A: 100 miles to 300 miles.
Notes: Tropospheric ducting traps microwave signals in temperature inversion layers, allowing them to travel well beyond normal line-of-sight distances. Under typical ducting conditions, this extended range is about 100 miles to 300 miles for amateur radio microwave communication.
E3A12: Q: What is most likely to result in auroral propagation? A: Severe geomagnetic storms.
Notes: Severe geomagnetic storms disturb the Earth's magnetosphere and upper atmosphere, creating auroral ionization that can refract and scatter radio waves. This enhanced ionization along auroral zones enables unique VHF and sometimes HF propagation paths useful to amateur radio operators.
E3A13: Q: Which of these emission modes is best for auroral propagation? A: CW.
Notes: Auroral propagation distorts and scatters signals, and CW’s narrow bandwidth and simple on-off keying make it more likely to be intelligible under these rapid fading and flutter conditions. For this reason, CW is preferred for making reliable contacts via aurora in the Amateur Extra exam context.
E3A14: Q: What are circularly polarized electromagnetic waves? A: Waves with rotating electric and magnetic fields.
Notes: In circular polarization, the electric and magnetic field vectors rotate in a circular manner as the wave propagates. This rotation defines the polarization type and is important for matching antenna polarization in advanced amateur radio communication links.

E3B12: Q: What is chordal-hop propagation? A: Successive ionospheric refractions without an intermediate reflection from the ground.
Notes: In chordal-hop propagation, the radio signal refracts multiple times between ionospheric layers while staying above the Earth’s surface, so it never reflects off the ground between hops. E3B13: Q: What type of polarization is supported by ground-wave propagation? A: Vertical.

### Group E4C - Receiver noise, sensitivity, and dynamic range
E4C01: Q: What is an effect of excessive phase noise in an SDR receiver’s master clock oscillator? A: It can combine with strong signals on nearby frequencies to generate interference.
Notes: In an SDR receiver, excessive phase noise spreads the energy of nearby strong signals, causing them to mix and produce interference products. This degrades receiver performance by raising the noise floor and masking weaker signals on or near the desired frequency.
E4C02: Q: Which of the following receiver circuits can be effective in eliminating interference from strong out-of-band signals? A: A front-end filter or preselector.
Notes: Strong signals outside the desired band can overload the receiver front end, causing distortion and intermodulation. A front-end filter or preselector limits the receiver input to the desired frequency range, reducing the impact of strong out-of-band signals.
E4C03: Q: What is the term for the suppression in an FM receiver of one signal by another stronger signal on the same frequency? A: Capture effect.
Notes: FM receivers tend to lock onto and demodulate the stronger of two signals on the same frequency, effectively suppressing the weaker one. This phenomenon is known in radio communications as the capture effect and is a characteristic behavior of FM detection circuits.
E4C04: Q: What is the noise figure of a receiver? A: The ratio in dB of the noise generated by the receiver to the theoretical minimum noise.
Notes: Noise figure quantifies how much additional noise a receiver adds compared to an ideal, noise-free system at the same temperature and bandwidth. In amateur radio, a lower noise figure means the receiver can detect weaker signals more effectively above the theoretical noise floor.
E4C05: Q: What does a receiver noise floor of -174 dBm represent? A: The theoretical noise in a 1 Hz bandwidth at the input of a perfect receiver at room temperature.
Notes: -174 dBm in 1 Hz bandwidth is the fundamental thermal noise limit set by physics for a receiver at room temperature. It provides a reference point for comparing real receiver noise performance in amateur radio systems.
E4C06: Q: How much does increasing a receiver’s bandwidth from 50 Hz to 1,000 Hz increase the receiver’s noise floor? A: 13 dB.
Notes: Receiver noise power is proportional to bandwidth, so increasing the bandwidth from 50 Hz to 1,000 Hz significantly increases the integrated noise. This bandwidth ratio corresponds to a 13 dB increase in the noise floor, which reduces the system’s ability to copy weak signals.
E4C07: Q: What does the MDS of a receiver represent? A: The minimum discernible signal.
Notes: In amateur radio receiver specifications, minimum discernible signal (MDS) is the smallest signal level that can be detected above the receiver’s noise floor. It is a key sensitivity measure that indicates how weak a signal the receiver can reliably hear under standard conditions.
E4C08: Q: An SDR receiver is overloaded when input signals exceed what level? A: The reference voltage of the analog-to-digital converter.
Notes: In an SDR, the analog-to-digital converter (ADC) sets the maximum signal level it can accurately digitize. When the input exceeds the ADC reference voltage, the signal clips and causes overload distortion in the received signal.
E4C09: Q: Which of the following choices is a good reason for selecting a high IF for a superheterodyne HF or VHF communications receiver? A: Easier for front-end circuitry to eliminate image responses.
Notes: Using a higher IF places image frequencies farther away from the desired signal, making them easier to reject with practical RF filtering at the receiver front end. This improves receiver performance by reducing unwanted signals that could otherwise mix into the passband.
E4C10: Q: What is an advantage of having a variety of receiver bandwidths from which to select? A: Receive bandwidth can be set to match the modulation bandwidth, maximizing signal-to-noise ratio and minimizing interference.
Notes: When the receiver bandwidth closely matches the signal’s modulation bandwidth, less noise and unwanted signals are admitted, improving intelligibility. This is important in amateur radio to optimize reception under varying band conditions and signal types.
E4C11: Q: Why does input attenuation reduce receiver overload on the lower frequency HF bands with little or no impact on signal-to-noise ratio? A: Atmospheric noise is generally greater than internally generated noise even after attenuation.
Notes: On the lower HF bands, the dominant noise source is atmospheric noise, not the receiver’s own internal noise. Adding attenuation lowers both the signal and the strong incoming noise, but since atmospheric noise still remains stronger than the receiver noise, the signal-to-noise ratio is essentially unchanged while overload is reduced.
E4C12: Q: How does a narrow-band roofing filter affect receiver performance? A: It improves blocking dynamic range by attenuating strong signals near the receive frequency.
Notes: By sharply limiting the bandwidth early in the receiver, a narrow-band roofing filter reduces the energy from strong nearby signals that could otherwise overload later stages. This improves the receiver’s ability to handle strong adjacent signals without degrading reception of the desired signal, which is described as better blocking dynamic range in the Amateur Extra exam context.
E4C13: Q: What is reciprocal mixing? A: Local oscillator phase noise mixing with adjacent strong signals to create interference to desired signals.
Notes: In a receiver, phase noise from the local oscillator can mix with nearby strong signals and produce noise in the passband of the desired weak signal. This degrades the effective sensitivity and dynamic range of the receiver, which is described as reciprocal mixing in the Amateur Extra exam context.
E4C14: Q: What is the purpose of the receiver IF Shift control? A: To reduce interference from stations transmitting on adjacent frequencies.
Notes: By shifting the intermediate frequency passband, the IF Shift control moves interfering signals from nearby frequencies outside the receiver’s most sensitive passband. This helps the operator better copy the desired signal when strong adjacent-channel signals are present.

### Group E4D - Receiver linearity, interference, and link margin
E4D01: Q: What is meant by the blocking dynamic range of a receiver? A: The difference in dB between the noise floor and the level of an incoming signal that will cause 1 dB of gain compression.
Notes: Blocking dynamic range describes how strong a nearby signal can be before it starts to compress the receiver’s gain by 1 dB. In Amateur Extra exam context, this measures how well a receiver can handle strong signals without degrading the ability to copy weaker signals near the noise floor.
E4D02: Q: Which of the following describes problems caused by poor dynamic range in a receiver? A: Spurious signals caused by cross modulation and desensitization from strong adjacent signals.
Notes: Dynamic range defines how well a receiver can handle both weak and strong signals at the same time. When dynamic range is poor, strong nearby signals overload the receiver front end, creating spurious signals and reducing sensitivity to weaker signals (desensitization).
E4D03: Q: What creates intermodulation interference between two repeaters in close proximity? A: The output signals mix in the final amplifier of one or both transmitters.
Notes: When strong RF signals from nearby repeaters are present at a transmitter’s final amplifier, they can mix nonlinearly and generate new, unwanted intermodulation products. These products can fall on other frequencies and cause interference, which is why close-proximity repeaters must be carefully engineered to avoid this condition.
E4D06: Q: What is the term for the reduction in receiver sensitivity caused by a strong signal near the received frequency? A: Desensitization.
Notes: A strong nearby signal can partially overload the receiver front end, effectively reducing its ability to detect weaker signals on or near the desired frequency. This loss of effective sensitivity is called desensitization in amateur radio receiver design and operation.
E4D07: Q: Which of the following reduces the likelihood of receiver desensitization? A: Insert attenuation before the first RF stage.
Notes: Adding attenuation ahead of the first RF amplifier reduces very strong incoming signals to a manageable level, preventing the receiver front end from overloading. This keeps the receiver more linear and preserves sensitivity to weaker signals in crowded Amateur Extra band conditions.
E4D08: Q: What causes intermodulation in an electronic circuit? A: Nonlinear circuits or devices.
Notes: When a circuit or device is nonlinear, it mixes two or more signals to produce additional signals at sum and difference frequencies of the originals. These new, unwanted frequencies are called intermodulation products and can cause interference in amateur radio systems.
E4D09: Q: What is the purpose of the preselector in a communications receiver? A: To increase the rejection of signals outside the band being received.
Notes: A preselector is a tunable filter placed ahead of the receiver's front end that passes only the desired frequency range. By rejecting out-of-band signals, it reduces overload and interference, improving reception of the intended amateur band signals.
E4D10: Q: What does a third-order intercept level of 40 dBm mean with respect to receiver performance? A: A pair of 40 dBm input signals will theoretically generate a third-order intermodulation product that has the same output amplitude as either of the input signals.
Notes: The third-order intercept point is a theoretical measure of how strong signals can be before third-order intermodulation products reach the same level as the desired signals. A 40 dBm intercept level indicates the receiver can better tolerate strong nearby signals before intermodulation distortion becomes significant, which is important for crowded amateur bands.
E4D11: Q: Why are odd-order intermodulation products, created within a receiver, of particular interest compared to other products? A: Odd-order products of two signals in the band being received are also likely to be within the band.
Notes: In the Extra exam context, odd-order intermodulation products can fall inside the same band as the desired signals, where they directly interfere with reception. This makes them more problematic than products that fall outside the tuned band and are more easily filtered out.
E4D12: Q: What is the link margin in a system with a transmit power level of 10 W (+40 dBm), a system antenna gain of 10 dBi, a cable loss of 3 dB, a path loss of 136 dB, a receiver minimum discernable signal of -103 dBm, and a required signal-to-noise ratio of 6 dB? A: +8dB.

In this case the calculation yields a link margin of +8 dB, indicating the signal is 8 dB stronger than the minimum required for adequate reception.
E4D13: Q: What is the received signal level with a transmit power of 10 W (+40 dBm), a transmit antenna gain of 6 dBi, a receive antenna gain of 3 dBi, and a path loss of 100 dB? A: -51 dBm.
Notes: In link budget calculations, you add the transmit power (in dBm) and both antenna gains (in dBi), then subtract the path loss (in dB), giving +40 dBm + 6 dBi + 3 dBi − 100 dB = -51 dBm. This result represents the expected received signal level under those specified path and antenna conditions.
E4D14: Q: What power level does a receiver minimum discernible signal of -100 dBm represent? A: 0.1 picowatts.
Notes: In RF engineering, dBm is a logarithmic measure of power referenced to 1 milliwatt, and -100 dBm converts mathematically to 0.1 picowatts. This very low power level illustrates the extreme sensitivity required of receivers in weak-signal Amateur Extra class operation scenarios.

## SUBELEMENT E5 - Core Electrical Principles
### Group E5A - Resonance, Q, and bandwidth behavior
E5A01: Q: What can cause the voltage across reactances in a series RLC circuit to be higher than the voltage applied to the entire circuit? A: Resonance.
Notes: At resonance in a series RLC circuit, the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal in magnitude and cause large circulating currents, which produce higher voltages across the inductor and capacitor than the total applied voltage. This is known as voltage magnification at resonance and is an important effect in RF circuit design and analysis for the Amateur Extra exam.
E5A02: Q: What is the resonant frequency of an RLC circuit if R is 22 ohms, L is 50 microhenries, and C is 40 picofarads? A: 3.56 MHz.
Notes: For an RLC circuit, the resonant frequency is determined by the inductance and capacitance using the formula f = 1 / (2π√(LC)). Substituting L = 50 microhenries and C = 40 picofarads into this formula yields approximately 3.56 MHz.
E5A03: Q: What is the magnitude of the impedance of a series RLC circuit at resonance? A: Approximately equal to circuit resistance.
Notes: In a series RLC circuit at resonance, the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal in magnitude and cancel each other, leaving only the resistive component. Therefore, the circuit’s impedance magnitude is approximately the same as its resistance.
E5A04: Q: What is the magnitude of the impedance of a parallel RLC circuit at resonance? A: Approximately equal to circuit resistance.
Notes: At resonance in a parallel RLC circuit, the inductive and capacitive reactances cancel each other, leaving the impedance determined primarily by the resistance. Therefore, the total impedance magnitude at resonance closely matches the circuit resistance value.
E5A05: Q: What is the result of increasing the Q of an impedance-matching circuit? A: Matching bandwidth is decreased.
Notes: In an RF impedance-matching circuit, a higher Q means a narrower resonance curve, so the circuit effectively matches impedances over a smaller range of frequencies. This reduced bandwidth is a key tradeoff when designing high-Q matching networks for amateur radio applications.
E5A06: Q: What is the magnitude of the circulating current within the components of a parallel LC circuit at resonance? A: It is at a maximum.
Notes: At resonance in a parallel LC circuit, equal and opposite reactive currents flow between the inductor and capacitor, creating the largest possible circulating current. This high internal current occurs even though the net current from the source is minimal at resonance.
E5A07: Q: What is the magnitude of the current at the input of a parallel RLC circuit at resonance? A: Minimum.
Notes: At resonance in a parallel RLC circuit, the reactive currents in the inductor and capacitor cancel each other, so the source only supplies the small resistive current. This results in the minimum input current at resonance for a parallel RLC configuration.
E5A08: Q: What is the phase relationship between the current through and the voltage across a series resonant circuit at resonance? A: The voltage and current are in phase.
Notes: At series resonance, the inductive and capacitive reactances cancel, leaving a purely resistive impedance, so the current and voltage align in phase. This in-phase condition is a key indicator of resonance in a series RLC circuit used in Amateur Extra exam problems.
E5A09: Q: How is the Q of an RLC parallel resonant circuit calculated? A: Resistance divided by the reactance of either the inductance or capacitance.
Notes: In a parallel resonant circuit, Q is defined by the ratio of resistance to reactive impedance at resonance. Using the reactance of either the inductor or capacitor gives the same Q value because their reactances are equal in magnitude at resonance.
E5A10: Q: What is the resonant frequency of an RLC circuit if R is 33 ohms, L is 50 microhenries, and C is 10 picofarads? A: 7.12 MHz.
Notes: Resonant frequency in an RLC circuit is determined by the inductor and capacitor values using the formula fr = 1 / (2π√(LC)). Substituting L = 50 microhenries and C = 10 picofarads into this formula yields approximately 7.12 MHz.
E5A11: Q: What is the half-power bandwidth of a resonant circuit that has a resonant frequency of 7.1 MHz and a Q of 150? A: 47.3 kHz.
Notes: For a resonant circuit, the half-power bandwidth is found by dividing the resonant frequency by the Q, so 7.1 MHz ÷ 150 = 47.3 kHz. This relationship is commonly used in amateur radio to relate selectivity (bandwidth) to Q at a given operating frequency.
E5A12: Q: What is the half-power bandwidth of a resonant circuit that has a resonant frequency of 3.7 MHz and a Q of 118? A: 31.4 kHz.
Notes: For resonant circuits, the half-power bandwidth equals the resonant frequency divided by the quality factor, so 3.7 MHz ÷ 118 ≈ 31.4 kHz. This relationship between Q, resonant frequency, and bandwidth is fundamental in Extra-class filter and resonance problems.
E5A13: Q: What is an effect of increasing Q in a series resonant circuit? A: Internal voltages increase.
Notes: At series resonance, higher Q indicates lower losses and higher circulating currents in the reactive components, which increases the voltages developed across them. This effect is important in high-Q resonant circuits because internal voltages can become much larger than the applied source voltage.

### Group E5B - Time constants, reactance, phase, and admittance
E5B01: Q: What is the term for the time required for the capacitor in an RC circuit to be charged to 63.2% of the applied voltage or to discharge to 36.8% of its initial voltage? A: One time constant.
Notes: In an RC circuit, the time constant is a standard measure of how quickly the voltage across a capacitor changes, defined by these specific charge and discharge percentages. This concept is widely used in analyzing timing, filtering, and transient behavior in radio and electronic circuits.
E5B02: Q: What letter is commonly used to represent susceptance? A: B.
Notes: In the context of complex impedance and admittance, susceptance is the imaginary component of admittance and is conventionally denoted by the letter B in circuit equations and diagrams. This standard notation is used throughout electronics and amateur radio theory.
E5B03: Q: How is impedance in polar form converted to an equivalent admittance? A: Take the reciprocal of the magnitude and change the sign of the angle.
Notes: Admittance is the reciprocal of impedance, so in polar form you invert the magnitude and reverse the angle. This directly yields the equivalent admittance from the given impedance for circuit analysis on the Amateur Extra exam.
E5B04: Q: What is the time constant of a circuit having two 220-microfarad capacitors and two 1-megohm resistors, all in parallel? A: 220 seconds.
Notes: In a parallel circuit, the capacitances add to give 440 microfarads and the resistances combine to give 0.5 megohms, so the time constant τ = R × C = 0.5 × 10^6 ohms × 440 × 10^-6 farads = 220 seconds. This time constant describes how quickly voltage in the RC circuit charges or discharges toward its final value.
E5B05: Q: What is the effect on the magnitude of pure reactance when it is converted to susceptance? A: It is replaced by its reciprocal.
Notes: Susceptance is the reciprocal of reactance in AC circuit analysis, so converting from reactance to susceptance inverts the magnitude. This relationship is fundamental when switching between impedance and admittance representations in the Amateur Extra exam context.
E5B06: Q: What is susceptance? A: The imaginary part of admittance.
Notes: In AC circuit analysis, admittance is a complex quantity with a real part (conductance) and an imaginary part (susceptance), analogous to how impedance has resistance and reactance components. Susceptance therefore represents the reactive portion of how easily a circuit allows AC to flow, making it the imaginary part of admittance.
E5B07: Q: What is the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through a series RLC circuit if XC is 500 ohms, R is 1 kilohm, and XL is 250 ohms? A: 14.0 degrees with the voltage lagging the current.
Notes: The net reactance is the difference between inductive and capacitive reactance, and the phase angle is determined by the arctangent of the net reactance divided by the resistance. A net capacitive reactance causes the voltage to lag the current by 14.0 degrees in this case.
E5B08: Q: What is the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through a series RLC circuit if XC is 300 ohms, R is 100 ohms, and XL is 100 ohms? A: 63 degrees with the voltage lagging the current.
Notes: The net reactance is found from XL − XC, which is negative in this case, indicating a capacitive circuit where current leads voltage. Using the ratio of reactance to resistance gives a phase angle of 63 degrees, with the voltage lagging the current.
E5B09: Q: What is the relationship between the AC current through a capacitor and the voltage across a capacitor? A: Current leads voltage by 90 degrees.
Notes: In AC circuits, a capacitor’s current responds immediately to changes in voltage, causing the current waveform to reach its peaks a quarter cycle (90 degrees) before the voltage waveform. This 90-degree lead is a fundamental phase relationship used in analyzing reactive components on the Amateur Extra exam.
E5B10: Q: What is the relationship between the AC current through an inductor and the voltage across an inductor? A: Voltage leads current by 90 degrees.
Notes: Inductive reactance causes the induced voltage to reach its maximum value one-quarter cycle (90 degrees) before the current. This phase relationship is fundamental to analyzing AC circuits that include inductors in the Amateur Extra exam.
E5B11: Q: What is the phase angle between the voltage across and the current through a series RLC circuit if XC is 25 ohms, R is 100 ohms, and XL is 75 ohms? A: 27 degrees with the voltage leading the current.
Notes: The net reactance is XL - XC = 75 ohms - 25 ohms = 50 ohms, which is inductive, so the voltage leads the current. The phase angle is arctangent of the reactance divided by the resistance, arctan(50 / 100) ≈ 27 degrees, giving 27 degrees with the voltage leading the current.
E5B12: Q: What is admittance? A: The inverse of impedance.
Notes: In AC circuit analysis, admittance quantifies how easily current flows and is defined mathematically as the reciprocal of impedance. This relationship is fundamental when analyzing complex RF networks and matching circuits in amateur radio systems.

### Group E5C - Rectangular, polar, and phasor representations
E5C01: Q: Which of the following represents pure capacitive reactance of 100 ohms in rectangular notation? A: 0 - j100.
Notes: In rectangular form, a purely reactive impedance has zero real part and an imaginary part equal to the reactance, which is negative for a capacitor. Therefore, a 100-ohm capacitive reactance is written as 0 - j100.
E5C02: Q: How are impedances described in polar coordinates? A: By magnitude and phase angle.
Notes: In AC circuit analysis for amateur radio, impedance is often expressed in polar form so that its size (magnitude) and timing shift of the waveform (phase angle) are clear. This format makes it easier to analyze and combine impedances using phasor methods on the Extra exam.
E5C03: Q: Which of the following represents a pure inductive reactance in polar coordinates? A: A positive 90 degree phase angle.
Notes: Inductive reactance causes the current to lag the voltage by 90 degrees, which is represented as a +90 degree phase angle in polar form. In AC circuit analysis for the Extra exam, this positive quadrature relationship identifies a purely inductive reactance.
E5C04: Q: What type of Y-axis scale is most often used for graphs of circuit frequency response? A: Logarithmic.
Notes: A logarithmic Y-axis matches how voltage gain, power gain, and attenuation are commonly expressed in decibels, making circuit frequency response easier to interpret over wide dynamic ranges. This scale allows small and large changes in response to be seen clearly on a single graph.
E5C05: Q: What kind of diagram is used to show the phase relationship between impedances at a given frequency? A: Phasor diagram.
Notes: In AC circuit and impedance analysis, phasor diagrams graphically represent sinusoidal voltages and currents as rotating vectors, making their relative phase angles clear. This helps amateur radio operators visualize how different impedances interact at a specific operating frequency.
E5C06: Q: What does the impedance 50 - j25 ohms represent? A: 50 ohms resistance in series with 25 ohms capacitive reactance.
Notes: In rectangular form, the real part of an impedance is the resistance and the negative imaginary part is the capacitive reactance, so 50 - j25 ohms describes 50 ohms resistance in series with 25 ohms capacitive reactance. This is the standard way AC circuit impedances are expressed in complex notation for amateur radio analysis.
E5C07: Q: Where is the impedance of a pure resistance plotted on rectangular coordinates? A: On the horizontal axis.
Notes: In rectangular impedance plots, the horizontal axis represents the resistive component while the vertical axis represents the reactive component. A pure resistance has no reactance, so its impedance lies entirely on the horizontal axis.
E5C08: Q: What coordinate system is often used to display the phase angle of a circuit containing resistance, inductive, and/or capacitive reactance? A: Polar coordinates.
Notes: In AC circuit analysis, impedance and phase angle are conveniently represented as vectors (phasors) in polar coordinates, showing magnitude and angle. This makes it easy to visualize the phase relationships between voltage and current in reactive circuits for the Amateur Extra exam.
E5C09: Q: When using rectangular coordinates to graph the impedance of a circuit, what do the axes represent? A: The X axis represents the resistive component, and the Y axis represents the reactive component.
Notes: Impedance can be expressed as a complex number, with resistance as the real part and reactance as the imaginary part. On a rectangular graph, the real (resistive) part is plotted on the X axis and the imaginary (reactive) part is plotted on the Y axis, which matches standard complex-plane representation used in amateur radio circuit analysis.

### Group E5D - RF behavior, skin effect, and power
E5D01: Q: What is the result of conductor skin effect? A: Resistance increases as frequency increases because RF current flows closer to the surface.
Notes: At higher RF frequencies, current is forced into a thinner layer near the surface of the conductor, effectively reducing the cross-sectional area carrying current. This reduced effective area increases the conductor's resistance as frequency increases, which is important in high-frequency circuit and antenna design on the Amateur Extra exam.
E5D02: Q: Why is it important to keep lead lengths short for components used in circuits for VHF and above? A: To minimize inductive reactance.
Notes: At VHF and higher frequencies, even small lengths of wire behave like inductors, adding unwanted inductive reactance. Short leads reduce these parasitic inductive effects and help the circuit operate as intended at high frequencies.
E5D03: Q: What is the phase relationship between current and voltage for reactive power? A: They are 90 degrees out of phase.
Notes: Reactive power arises in inductive and capacitive circuits where energy is alternately stored and returned, causing current and voltage to be shifted in time. In AC analysis, this stored-and-released power is represented by a 90-degree phase difference between current and voltage.
E5D04: Q: Why are short connections used at microwave frequencies? A: To reduce phase shift along the connection.
Notes: At microwave frequencies, even small lengths of transmission line can introduce significant phase shift, which can degrade circuit performance. Keeping connections short minimizes this phase shift and helps maintain the intended signal timing and impedance characteristics in high-frequency amateur radio equipment.
E5D05: Q: What parasitic characteristic causes electrolytic capacitors to be unsuitable for use at RF? A: Inductance.
Notes: At radio frequencies, the internal inductance of an electrolytic capacitor becomes significant enough that it no longer behaves like a pure capacitor. This parasitic inductance limits their effectiveness in RF circuits, so other capacitor types are preferred for RF applications.
E5D06: Q: What parasitic characteristic creates an inductor’s self-resonance? A: Inter-turn capacitance.
Notes: The stray capacitance between the turns of an inductor forms a resonant LC circuit with the inductor’s own inductance. At the self-resonant frequency, this parasitic LC behavior dominates the inductor’s response, which is important in high-frequency amateur radio circuit design.
E5D07: Q: What combines to create the self-resonance of a component? A: The component’s nominal and parasitic reactance.
Notes: In real components, unavoidable stray inductance and capacitance form parasitic reactances that interact with the intended (nominal) reactance. At a particular frequency these reactances can cancel, creating a natural or self-resonant frequency important for accurate circuit behavior in Amateur Extra-level designs.
E5D08: Q: What is the primary cause of loss in film capacitors at RF? A: Skin effect.
Notes: At radio frequencies, current in the metalized electrodes of a film capacitor is forced toward the surface by skin effect, increasing effective resistance. This higher resistance leads to greater power dissipation and loss in the capacitor at RF.
E5D09: Q: What happens to reactive power in ideal inductors and capacitors? A: Energy is stored in magnetic or electric fields, but power is not dissipated.
Notes: Reactive components like ideal inductors and capacitors alternately store and release energy in their fields without converting it to heat or other losses. In AC circuit analysis for the Extra exam, this is why reactive power does not represent real power dissipation in ideal components.
E5D10: Q: As a conductor’s diameter increases, what is the effect on its electrical length? A: It increases.
Notes: A thicker conductor changes the distribution of current and fields around it, which increases its effective electrical length relative to its physical length. In antenna and transmission line design, this means that larger-diameter conductors behave as if they are electrically longer at a given frequency.
E5D11: Q: How much real power is consumed in a circuit consisting of a 100-ohm resistor in series with a 100-ohm inductive reactance drawing 1 ampere? A: 100 watts.
Notes: Only the resistive component consumes real power, calculated as I²R = (1 A)² × 100 ohms = 100 watts. The inductive reactance contributes only to reactive power, not to real power dissipation.
E5D12: Q: What is reactive power? A: Wattless, nonproductive power.
Notes: In AC circuits, reactive power represents energy that is alternately stored and released by inductors and capacitors, so it does not perform useful work. On the exam, this is identified as wattless, nonproductive power to distinguish it from real power that does actual work in a load.


## SUBELEMENT E6 - Electronic and RF Components
### Group E6A - Semiconductor physics, BJTs, and FETs
E6A01: Q: In what application is gallium arsenide used as a semiconductor material? A: In microwave circuits.
Notes: Gallium arsenide has high electron mobility and low noise characteristics, making it especially suitable for high-frequency operation in microwave circuits. In the Amateur Extra exam context, it is associated with RF components used at microwave frequencies.
E6A02: Q: Which of the following semiconductor materials contains excess free electrons? A: N-type.
Notes: In an N-type semiconductor, donor impurities add extra electrons to the crystal lattice, creating an excess of free electrons that act as the majority charge carriers. This electron surplus is what gives N-type material its characteristic electrical behavior used in diodes and transistors on the exam.
E6A03: Q: Why does a PN-junction diode not conduct current when reverse biased? A: Holes in P-type material and electrons in the N-type material are separated by the applied voltage, widening the depletion region.
Notes: In reverse bias, the electric field pushes majority carriers away from the junction, increasing the width of the depletion region. This widened depletion region greatly reduces carrier flow across the junction, so almost no current can pass in normal operation.
E6A04: Q: What is the name given to an impurity atom that adds holes to a semiconductor crystal structure? A: Acceptor impurity.
Notes: In semiconductor physics for amateur radio electronics, an acceptor impurity creates deficiency of electrons, producing mobile holes that enable P-type conductivity. This term identifies the dopant that accepts electrons and thereby increases the hole concentration in the crystal lattice.
E6A05: Q: How does DC input impedance at the gate of a field-effect transistor (FET) compare with that of a bipolar transistor? A: An FET has higher input impedance.
Notes: In a FET, the gate is insulated from the channel and draws essentially no DC current, so its input impedance is very high. In a bipolar transistor, the base-emitter junction conducts current like a diode, resulting in a lower DC input impedance.
E6A06: Q: What is the beta of a bipolar junction transistor? A: The change in collector current with respect to the change in base current.
Notes: In exam terminology, beta (β) is the small-signal current gain of a BJT, defined as how much the collector current changes for a given change in base current. This expresses the transistor’s amplification factor for current in normal operation.
E6A07: Q: Which of the following indicates that a silicon NPN junction transistor is biased on? A: Base-to-emitter voltage of approximately 0.6 volts to 0.7 volts.
Notes: In normal operation of a silicon NPN transistor, the base-emitter junction must be forward biased at about 0.6 to 0.7 volts for the transistor to conduct. This voltage range is a standard characteristic of silicon junctions used in amateur radio circuits and is tested on the Amateur Extra exam.
E6A08: Q: What is the term for the frequency at which the grounded-base current gain of a bipolar junction transistor has decreased to 0.7 of the gain obtainable at 1 kHz? A: Alpha cutoff frequency.
Notes: The alpha cutoff frequency is defined where the transistor’s grounded-base current gain (alpha) falls to 70% of its low-frequency (e.g., 1 kHz) value. This parameter characterizes the high-frequency performance limit of a BJT in grounded-base configuration for amateur radio and RF design work.
E6A09: Q: What is a depletion-mode field-effect transistor (FET)? A: An FET that exhibits a current flow between source and drain when no gate voltage is applied.
Notes: In a depletion-mode FET, the channel is normally on, so current flows from source to drain with zero gate bias. Applying a gate voltage then reduces or depletes this current, which is important to understand when analyzing FET biasing in Amateur Extra exam problems.
E6A10: Q: In Figure E6-1, which is the schematic symbol for an N-channel dual-gate MOSFET? A: 4.
Notes: An N-channel dual-gate MOSFET is identified by a field-effect transistor symbol with two separate gate connections on the symbol for a single N-channel device. In the exam’s Figure E6-1, symbol 4 corresponds to this dual-gate MOSFET configuration.
E6A11: Q: In Figure E6-1, which is the schematic symbol for a P-channel junction FET? A: 1.
Notes: A P-channel JFET is identified on schematics by its specific symbol shape and the orientation of the channel and gate relative to conventional current flow. In the exam’s Figure E6-1, symbol 1 matches the standard P-channel JFET symbol used in electronics diagrams.
E6A12: Q: What is the purpose of connecting Zener diodes between a MOSFET gate and its source or drain? A: To protect the gate from static damage.
Notes: A MOSFET gate is highly sensitive to overvoltage from static discharge because of its thin oxide layer. Zener diodes clamp excessive voltage, preventing damage and improving reliability in amateur radio circuits.

### Group E6B - Diode types, behavior, and applications
E6B01: Q: What is the most useful characteristic of a Zener diode? A: A constant voltage drop under conditions of varying current.
Notes: In the breakdown region, a Zener diode is designed to hold a nearly constant voltage across it even as the current through it changes. This property makes it useful for voltage regulation and reference circuits in amateur radio equipment.
E6B02: Q: Which characteristic of a Schottky diode makes it a better choice than a silicon junction diode for use as a power supply rectifier? A: Lower forward voltage drop.
Notes: In power supply rectifiers, a lower forward voltage drop reduces power loss and heat dissipation, improving efficiency. Schottky diodes typically have a significantly lower forward voltage than standard silicon junction diodes, making them preferable in this role.
E6B03: Q: What property of an LED's semiconductor material determines its forward voltage drop? A: Band gap.
Notes: In an LED, the band gap sets the energy of the photons produced when electrons recombine, which directly determines the forward voltage required for conduction and light emission. Different semiconductor band gaps therefore result in different forward voltage drops for LEDs used in amateur radio and other circuits.
E6B04: Q: What type of semiconductor device is designed for use as a voltage-controlled capacitor? A: Varactor diode.
Notes: A varactor diode changes its junction capacitance based on the applied reverse-bias voltage, allowing it to act as a tunable capacitor. This makes it useful in frequency-agile circuits such as VFOs, PLLs, and tuned RF stages in Amateur Extra-level designs.
E6B05: Q: What characteristic of a PIN diode makes it useful as an RF switch? A: Low junction capacitance.
Notes: Low junction capacitance allows a PIN diode to pass or block RF signals with minimal reactance, making it effective for switching at radio frequencies. This characteristic helps maintain signal integrity and reduces unwanted coupling in RF circuits used in Amateur Extra class applications.
E6B06: Q: Which of the following is a common use of a Schottky diode? A: As a VHF/UHF mixer or detector.
Notes: Schottky diodes have very fast switching speed and low forward voltage drop, which makes them well suited for mixing and detecting high-frequency VHF and UHF signals in amateur radio equipment. They are commonly found in receiver front ends and detector stages where low noise and high-frequency performance are important.
E6B07: Q: What causes a junction diode to fail from excessive current? A: Excessive junction temperature.
Notes: When too much current flows through a junction diode, power dissipation raises the junction temperature beyond its safe limit, damaging the semiconductor material. This thermal damage is what ultimately causes the diode to fail in amateur radio and other electronic circuits.
E6B08: Q: Which of the following is a Schottky barrier diode? A: Metal-semiconductor junction.
Notes: In a Schottky barrier diode, the rectifying action occurs at the interface between a metal and a semiconductor, rather than between two semiconductor regions. This metal-semiconductor junction gives the device its characteristic low forward voltage drop and fast switching behavior important in RF and high-speed circuits for the Amateur Extra exam context.
E6B09: Q: What is a common use for point-contact diodes? A: As an RF detector.
Notes: Point-contact diodes have very low junction capacitance and can rectify small RF signals, making them well suited for detecting or demodulating RF in receiver and test circuits. In the Amateur Extra exam context, they are classically associated with RF detection applications.
E6B10: Q: In Figure E6-2, which is the schematic symbol for a Schottky diode? A: 6.
Notes: On the Amateur Extra exam, the Schottky diode is identified by the standard diode symbol with distinctive added lines indicating its metal–semiconductor junction, which is shown as symbol 6 in Figure E6-2. Recognizing this symbol helps distinguish Schottky diodes from other diode types in circuit diagrams.
E6B11: Q: What is used to control the attenuation of RF signals by a PIN diode? A: Forward DC bias current.

### Group E6C - Digital IC families, logic, and PLDs
E6C01: Q: What is the function of hysteresis in a comparator? A: To prevent input noise from causing unstable output signals.
Notes: Hysteresis adds a small difference between the switching thresholds of a comparator so that minor noise or ripple on the input does not cause rapid, unwanted switching at the output. This stabilizes the output signal in practical radio and electronic circuits where noise is always present.
E6C02: Q: What happens when the level of a comparator’s input signal crosses the threshold voltage? A: The comparator changes its output state.
Notes: A comparator is designed to switch its output when the input voltage rises above or falls below a defined threshold voltage. This digital-like switching behavior is useful in amateur radio circuits for detecting signal levels and creating control or triggering signals.
E6C03: Q: What is tri-state logic? A: Logic devices with 0, 1, and high-impedance output states.
Notes: In Amateur Extra exam digital logic context, tri-state outputs allow a device to place its output line in a high-impedance state so it does not drive the bus, in addition to normal logic 0 and logic 1 levels. E6C04: Q: Which of the following is an advantage of BiCMOS logic? A: It has the high input impedance of CMOS and the low output impedance of bipolar transistors.
Notes: BiCMOS logic combines CMOS and bipolar technologies so that circuit inputs draw very little current while outputs can drive loads strongly. This combination is beneficial in RF and digital interface stages where minimal loading and strong signal drive are both important.
E6C05: Q: Which of the following digital logic families has the lowest power consumption? A: CMOS.
Notes: CMOS logic uses complementary MOSFET structures that draw very little static current, so its overall power consumption is lower than other logic families. This makes CMOS well suited for low-power and battery-operated amateur radio equipment and digital circuits.
E6C06: Q: Why do CMOS digital integrated circuits have high immunity to noise on the input signal or power supply? A: The input switching threshold is about half the power supply voltage.
Notes: With the switching threshold set near half the supply voltage, small noise fluctuations are unlikely to move the input across the threshold, so the logic state remains stable. This makes CMOS inputs and their associated logic levels more resistant to typical RF and power-supply noise encountered in amateur radio equipment.
E6C07: Q: What best describes a pull-up or pull-down resistor? A: A resistor connected to the positive or negative supply used to establish a voltage when an input or output is an open circuit.
Notes: Digital and logic circuits in amateur radio equipment use pull-up or pull-down resistors so that inputs and outputs assume a known logic level instead of floating when nothing is actively driving the line. This prevents unpredictable behavior and noise from affecting circuit operation.

### Group E6D - Inductors, transformers, and piezoelectric devices
E6D01: Q: What is piezoelectricity? A: A characteristic of materials that generate a voltage when stressed and that flex when a voltage is applied.
Notes: Certain crystals and ceramics used in amateur radio, such as in microphones and frequency-control devices, exhibit piezoelectricity by converting mechanical stress to voltage and electrical voltage to mechanical motion. E6D03: Q: Which of the following is an aspect of the piezoelectric effect? A: Mechanical deformation of material due to the application of a voltage.

E6D04: Q: Why are cores of inductors and transformers sometimes constructed of thin layers? A: To reduce power loss from eddy currents in the core.

E6D05: Q: How do ferrite and powdered iron compare for use in an inductor core? A: Ferrite cores generally require fewer turns to produce a given inductance value.

E6D11: Q: Which type of core material decreases inductance when inserted into a coil? A: Brass.

### Group E7A - Digital logic circuits and frequency dividers
E7A01: Q: Which circuit is bistable? A: A flip-flop.
Notes: A flip-flop has two stable output states and remains in one of those states until it is triggered to change, which is the defining characteristic of a bistable circuit in digital and radio-frequency control logic. In amateur radio equipment, flip-flops are commonly used for functions such as frequency dividers, state control, and digital sequencing.
E7A02: Q: What is the function of a decade counter? A: It produces one output pulse for every 10 input pulses.
Notes: In amateur radio and digital electronics, a decade counter is a frequency divider that counts input pulses and generates one output pulse for every group of 10 received. This is useful for frequency division, timing, and driving digital displays in radio equipment.
E7A03: Q: Which of the following can divide the frequency of a pulse train by 2? A: A flip-flop.
Notes: A flip-flop changes state on each input pulse, so its output frequency is half the input pulse frequency. This makes it a standard digital building block for frequency division in radio and signal processing circuits on the Amateur Extra exam.
E7A04: Q: How many flip-flops are required to divide a signal frequency by 16? A: 4.
Notes: Each flip-flop in a binary divider divides the input frequency by 2, so 4 flip-flops divide the signal by 2^4, which equals 16. This is a common technique in digital frequency division used in amateur radio circuits.
E7A05: Q: Which of the following circuits continuously alternates between two states without an external clock signal? A: Astable multivibrator.
Notes: In amateur radio and digital electronics, an astable multivibrator is used as a free-running oscillator because it repeatedly switches between two output levels on its own. This behavior provides a continuous square-wave signal without any external timing source.
E7A06: Q: What is a characteristic of a monostable multivibrator? A: It switches temporarily to an alternate state for a set time.
Notes: In amateur radio and other electronics applications, a monostable multivibrator is used as a one-shot timer that, when triggered, changes state for a defined time interval and then returns to its stable state. This timed pulse behavior makes it useful for shaping signals, keying circuits, or generating precise timing events in radio equipment.
E7A07: Q: What logical operation does a NAND gate perform? A: It produces a 0 at its output only if all inputs are 1.
Notes: A NAND gate is the inverse of an AND gate, so it outputs 0 only when every input is 1 and outputs 1 in all other input conditions. In amateur radio digital and logic circuits, NAND gates are commonly used because this behavior allows flexible implementation of complex logic functions.
E7A08: Q: What logical operation does an OR gate perform? A: It produces a 1 at its output if any input is 1.
Notes: In digital logic used in amateur radio equipment and signal processing, an OR gate outputs a logic 1 when one or more of its inputs are at logic 1. This behavior is fundamental to combining control signals and conditions in radio circuits and digital systems.
E7A09: Q: What logical operation is performed by a two-input exclusive NOR gate? A: It produces a 0 at its output if one and only one of its inputs is 1.
Notes: An exclusive NOR gate outputs 0 when the two inputs are different, which occurs when one and only one input is 1 in digital logic used in radio circuits. This behavior is important in digital communication and control systems found in advanced amateur radio equipment.
E7A10: Q: What is a truth table? A: A list of inputs and corresponding outputs for a digital device.
Notes: In digital electronics used in amateur radio equipment, a truth table systematically shows how each possible combination of logic inputs produces a specific logic output. This helps in analyzing and designing digital circuits such as logic gates, encoders, and decoders found in modern radios.
E7A11: Q: What does “positive logic” mean in reference to logic devices? A: High voltage represents a 1, low voltage a 0.
Notes: Digital circuits use voltage levels to represent binary states, and in positive logic the higher voltage level is defined as the active or '1' state. This convention is common in amateur radio digital and control circuitry, simplifying design and interpretation of logic signals.

### Group E7B - Amplifier classes, design, and distortion control
E7B01: Q: For what portion of the signal cycle does each active element in a push-pull, Class AB amplifier conduct? A: More than 180 degrees but less than 360 degrees.
Notes: Class AB operation is defined by conduction that extends slightly beyond half of the RF cycle but not for the full cycle, improving linearity while reducing distortion and power dissipation compared to other classes. This conduction angle characteristic is essential for linear amplification in many amateur radio RF stages.
E7B02: Q: What is a Class D amplifier? A: An amplifier that uses switching technology to achieve high efficiency.
Notes: In a Class D amplifier, the active devices operate as switches rather than linear elements, which greatly reduces power dissipation and results in high efficiency. This switching operation is why Class D designs are favored where efficiency and reduced heat are important, such as in modern RF and audio applications.
E7B03: Q: What circuit is required at the output of an RF switching amplifier? A: A filter to remove harmonic content.
Notes: RF switching amplifiers generate significant harmonic energy due to their non-linear operation. A filter at the output is needed to suppress these harmonics so the transmitted signal stays within its assigned bandwidth and meets FCC spectral purity requirements.
E7B04: Q: What is the operating point of a Class A common emitter amplifier? A: Approximately halfway between saturation and cutoff.
Notes: In a Class A common emitter amplifier, the transistor is biased so it remains active over the entire input signal cycle, which is achieved by placing the operating point midway between saturation and cutoff. This midpoint biasing allows maximum undistorted output swing for the amplified RF or audio signal.
E7B05: Q: What can be done to prevent unwanted oscillations in an RF power amplifier? A: Install parasitic suppressors and/or neutralize the stage.
Notes: Parasitic suppressors damp high-frequency unintended resonances, while neutralization cancels feedback that could cause self-oscillation in RF amplifier stages. Together, these techniques stabilize the amplifier so it only amplifies the intended signal for Amateur Extra-class operation.
E7B06: Q: What is a characteristic of a grounded-grid amplifier? A: Low input impedance.
Notes: In a grounded-grid amplifier, the input is applied between the cathode and ground, which presents a low impedance to the driving stage. This low input impedance makes it suitable for use with low-impedance drivers in high-frequency and high-power amateur radio applications.
E7B07: Q: Which of the following is the likely result of using a Class C amplifier to amplify a single-sideband phone signal? A: Signal distortion and excessive bandwidth.
Notes: Class C amplifiers are highly non-linear and clip the SSB waveform, which distorts the signal. This non-linearity spreads the signal’s energy over a wider frequency range, causing excessive bandwidth and interference outside the intended channel.
E7B08: Q: Why are switching amplifiers more efficient than linear amplifiers? A: The switching device is at saturation or cutoff most of the time.
Notes: In a switching amplifier, the active device is either fully on (saturation) or fully off (cutoff), so it dissipates very little power as heat. This high-efficiency operation is useful in amateur radio transmitters where minimizing wasted power and heat is important.
E7B09: Q: What is characteristic of an emitter follower (or common collector) amplifier? A: Input and output signals in-phase.
Notes: In an emitter follower (common collector) configuration, the output is taken from the emitter, which follows the input voltage at the base without inverting it. This means the output signal has the same phase as the input signal, an important characteristic for buffering and impedance matching in amateur radio circuits.

Notes: R1 and R2 form a voltage divider that sets a stable DC bias voltage at the transistor’s base or gate so the amplifier operates in the desired region of its characteristic curve. This biasing method helps maintain consistent performance despite variations in device parameters or supply voltage.
E7B11: Q: In Figure E7-1, what is the purpose of R3? A: Self bias.

### Group E7C - Filters, matching networks, and their applications
E7C01: Q: How are the capacitors and inductors of a low-pass filter Pi-network arranged between the network’s input and output? A: A capacitor is connected between the input and ground, another capacitor is connected between the output and ground, and an inductor is connected between the input and output.
Notes: This arrangement forms the classic Pi-network, where the shunt capacitors to ground and the series inductor between input and output attenuate higher frequencies while passing lower frequencies. It is commonly used in amateur radio to provide low-pass filtering for transmitters and other RF stages.
E7C02: Q: What is the frequency response of a T-network with series capacitors and a shunt inductor? A: High-pass.
Notes: In this T-network, the series capacitors increasingly pass higher-frequency signals while the shunt inductor presents lower impedance at low frequencies, attenuating them. This combination results in a high-pass response, which is useful in RF filtering applications for the Amateur Extra exam context.
E7C03: Q: What is the purpose of adding an inductor to a Pi-network to create a Pi-L-network? A: Greater harmonic suppression.
Notes: The extra series inductor in a Pi-L-network improves filtering of unwanted harmonic frequencies beyond the fundamental signal. This results in cleaner transmitted signals that better meet FCC spectral purity requirements for Amateur Extra operators.
E7C04: Q: How does an impedance-matching circuit transform a complex impedance to a resistive impedance? A: It cancels the reactive part of the impedance and changes the resistive part to the desired value.
Notes: In RF circuits, impedance-matching networks use reactive components to neutralize (cancel) the unwanted reactance so only resistance remains. The circuit is then configured so that the remaining resistance equals the desired value for maximum power transfer and proper system matching in amateur radio applications.
E7C05: Q: Which filter type has ripple in the passband and a sharp cutoff? A: A Chebyshev filter.
Notes: In amateur radio equipment design, a Chebyshev filter is chosen when a sharper transition between passband and stopband is needed, even though it introduces ripple within the passband. This characteristic makes it useful in RF and IF stages where steep selectivity is more important than a perfectly flat passband response.
E7C06: Q: What are the characteristics of an elliptical filter? A: Extremely sharp cutoff with one or more notches in the stop band.
Notes: Elliptical filters are designed for very selective RF applications, providing rapid attenuation beyond the passband. The additional notches in the stop band further suppress unwanted signals at specific frequencies, improving interference rejection for Amateur Extra level designs.
E7C07: Q: Which describes a Pi-L network? A: A Pi-network with an additional output series inductor.
Notes: In transmitter output matching circuits, a Pi-L network adds a series inductor at the output of a standard Pi-network. This extra inductor improves impedance matching and harmonic suppression for Amateur Extra class RF designs.
E7C08: Q: Which of the following is most frequently used as a band-pass or notch filter in VHF and UHF transceivers? A: A helical filter.
Notes: Helical filters provide sharp, tunable band-pass or notch characteristics at VHF and UHF frequencies in a compact form. This makes them well suited for selectivity and interference rejection in modern VHF/UHF transceiver front ends and IF stages.
E7C09: Q: What is a crystal lattice filter? A: A filter for low-level signals made using quartz crystals.
Notes: Quartz crystals provide very stable and narrow frequency-selective characteristics, making them ideal for filtering low-level signals in receiver IF stages. In the Amateur Extra exam context, this identifies the specific type of filter construction used in precise RF signal processing.
E7C10: Q: Which of the following filters is used in a 2-meter band repeater duplexer? A: A cavity filter.
Notes: A cavity filter provides the narrow, high-Q filtering needed in a 2-meter repeater duplexer to separate the transmitter and receiver frequencies. This high isolation prevents the strong transmitter signal from overloading the sensitive receiver on the same antenna system.
E7C11: Q: Which of the following measures a filter’s ability to reject signals in adjacent channels? A: Shape factor.
Notes: Shape factor compares a filter’s bandwidth at two different attenuation levels, indicating how sharply it transitions from the passband to the stopband. In Amateur Extra exam context, it is the standard figure of merit for how well a filter rejects nearby interfering signals on adjacent channels.

### Group E7E - Modulators, demodulators, detectors, and mixers
E7E01: Q: Which of the following can be used to generate FM phone signals? A: Reactance modulation of a local oscillator.
Notes: In reactance modulation, a circuit varies the reactance (capacitance or inductance) seen by a local oscillator to change its frequency in accordance with the audio signal, producing frequency modulation (FM). This method is commonly used in amateur radio transmitters to generate FM phone emissions for the Amateur Extra exam context.
E7E02: Q: What is the function of a reactance modulator? A: Produce PM or FM signals by varying a capacitance.
Notes: By varying an effective capacitance in the tuned circuit, a reactance modulator changes the resonant frequency or phase of the RF signal, creating PM or FM. This method is commonly used in amateur radio transmitters to generate stable angle-modulated signals for the Amateur Extra exam context.
E7E03: Q: What is a frequency discriminator? A: A circuit for detecting FM signals.
Notes: In FM receivers, a frequency discriminator converts the instantaneous frequency variations of the FM signal into corresponding amplitude variations, allowing the original modulating information to be recovered. This function is essential for demodulating frequency-modulated signals in amateur radio communications equipment.
E7E04: Q: What is one way to produce a single-sideband phone signal? A: Use a balanced modulator followed by a filter.
Notes: In SSB generation, a balanced modulator suppresses the carrier and produces a double-sideband signal, and a filter then removes the unwanted sideband to leave a single-sideband phone signal. This method is a standard SSB generation technique used in amateur radio transmitters for efficient, narrowband voice communication.
E7E05: Q: What is added to an FM speech channel to boost the higher audio frequencies? A: A pre-emphasis network.
Notes: In FM communication, a pre-emphasis network increases the level of higher audio frequencies before modulation to improve signal-to-noise performance. E7E06: Q: Why is de-emphasis used in FM communications receivers? A: For compatibility with transmitters using phase modulation.
Notes: Phase-modulated transmitters use pre-emphasis to shape the audio, so FM receivers apply de-emphasis to restore a flat audio response. This complementary process ensures proper audio fidelity and standardized performance in FM communication systems.
E7E07: Q: What is meant by the term “baseband” in radio communications? A: The frequency range occupied by a message signal prior to modulation.

Understanding baseband is essential because modulation schemes operate on this original message signal to place it onto an RF carrier for communication.
E7E08: Q: What are the principal frequencies that appear at the output of a mixer? A: The two input frequencies along with their sum and difference frequencies.
Notes: A mixer is a nonlinear device that produces new frequencies equal to the sum and difference of the original input signals while also passing the original input frequencies. In amateur radio equipment, these sum and difference products are used for frequency conversion such as in superheterodyne receivers and transmitters.
E7E09: Q: What occurs when the input signal levels to a mixer are too high? A: Spurious mixer products are generated.
Notes: Excessive input levels drive the mixer into nonlinear operation, creating unwanted mixing products. These spurious signals can cause interference and degrade receiver or transmitter performance in amateur radio systems.
E7E10: Q: How does a diode envelope detector function? A: By rectification and filtering of RF signals.
Notes: In an AM receiver, the diode envelope detector rectifies the RF signal to produce a unidirectional waveform and then filters it to recover the audio-frequency envelope. This process extracts the modulation information from the RF carrier for further amplification and listening.
E7E11: Q: Which type of detector is used for demodulating SSB signals? A: Product detector.
Notes: A product detector mixes the incoming SSB signal with a beat-frequency oscillator (BFO) signal to reinsert the missing carrier and recover the original audio. This method provides proper demodulation and intelligible audio for single sideband reception in amateur radio equipment.

### Group E7F - SDR basics, DSP, and data conversion
E7F01: Q: What is meant by “direct sampling” in software defined radios? A: Incoming RF is digitized by an analog-to-digital converter without being mixed with a local oscillator signal.
Notes: In a direct sampling SDR, the antenna’s RF signal is converted straight into digital form at RF frequencies, eliminating the need for analog mixing stages. This allows the radio’s tuning and filtering to be performed primarily in software instead of using traditional superheterodyne circuitry.
E7F02: Q: What kind of digital signal processing audio filter is used to remove unwanted noise from a received SSB signal? A: An adaptive filter.
Notes: Adaptive filters continuously adjust their response to the characteristics of the incoming signal and noise, allowing them to effectively reduce noise in SSB reception. In the Amateur Extra exam context, this identifies the specific DSP technique applied to clean up received audio.
E7F03: Q: What type of digital signal processing filter is used to generate an SSB signal? A: A Hilbert-transform filter.
Notes: In DSP-based SSB generation, a Hilbert-transform filter produces a 90-degree phase shift required to separate and suppress one sideband. This phase-shift method enables clean SSB generation without needing analog filters for sideband selection.
E7F04: Q: Which method generates an SSB signal using digital signal processing? A: Signals are combined in quadrature phase relationship.
Notes: In DSP-based SSB generation, two versions of the audio or baseband signal are processed 90 degrees out of phase (in quadrature) and then combined so that one sideband is reinforced while the other and the carrier are canceled. This quadrature technique digitally implements the phasing method of SSB generation used in modern transceivers.
E7F05: Q: How frequently must an analog signal be sampled to be accurately reproduced? A: At least twice the rate of the highest frequency component of the signal.
Notes: This is the Nyquist sampling criterion, which ensures that all frequency components of the analog signal can be reconstructed without aliasing. In amateur radio digital systems, sampling at twice the highest audio or RF baseband frequency preserves the original signal information.
E7F06: Q: What is the minimum number of bits required to sample a signal with a range of 1 volt at a resolution of 1 millivolt? A: 10 bits.
Notes: A 1-volt range with 1-millivolt resolution requires 1000 distinct steps, and 10 bits provide 2^10 = 1024 levels, which is sufficient. In Amateur Extra digital and DSP applications, bit depth must be high enough to cover the full voltage range at the desired resolution.
E7F07: Q: What function is performed by a Fast Fourier Transform? A: Converting signals from the time domain to the frequency domain.
Notes: In amateur radio and signal processing, the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is an algorithm that efficiently analyzes how signal energy is distributed across different frequencies. This time-to-frequency conversion is fundamental for spectrum analysis, digital signal processing, and many modern radio applications tested on the Amateur Extra exam.
E7F08: Q: What is the function of decimation? A: Reducing the effective sample rate by removing samples.
Notes: In digital signal processing for amateur radio, decimation reduces the data rate by systematically discarding samples while preserving the essential information in the signal. This lower effective sample rate eases processing requirements in receivers and other DSP-based systems.
E7F09: Q: Why is an anti-aliasing filter required in a decimator? A: It removes high-frequency signal components that would otherwise be reproduced as lower frequency components.
Notes: When you decimate (reduce the sampling rate), any signal components above the new Nyquist frequency will fold back into the passband as false low-frequency signals (aliasing). The anti-aliasing filter prevents this by removing those high-frequency components before decimation in a DSP-based amateur radio system.
E7F10: Q: What aspect of receiver analog-to-digital conversion determines the maximum receive bandwidth of a direct-sampling software defined radio (SDR)? A: Sample rate.
Notes: According to sampling theory, the analog-to-digital converter can only accurately represent signals up to a bandwidth related to its sample rate. A higher sample rate allows the SDR to process a wider slice of RF spectrum at once, setting the maximum receive bandwidth.
E7F11: Q: What sets the minimum detectable signal level for a direct-sampling software defined receiver in the absence of atmospheric or thermal noise? A: Reference voltage level and sample width in bits.
Notes: In a direct-sampling SDR, the analog-to-digital converter (ADC) dynamic range is determined by its reference voltage and number of bits, which together define the smallest voltage step it can resolve. With no external noise present, this quantization limit sets the minimum detectable signal level for the receiver.
E7F12: Q: Which of the following is generally true of Finite Impulse Response (FIR) filters? A: FIR filters can delay all frequency components of the signal by the same amount.
Notes: This property means an FIR filter can provide linear phase response, so all frequency components experience the same time delay. In Amateur Extra-level DSP applications, this preserves waveform shape, which is important for maintaining signal integrity in modulation and demodulation processes.
E7F13: Q: What is the function of taps in a digital signal processing filter? A: Provide incremental signal delays for filter algorithms.
Notes: Each tap corresponds to a delayed version of the input signal that is individually weighted and summed to implement the desired filter characteristics. These incremental delays are essential to performing the mathematical convolution that defines the DSP filter’s operation.
E7F14: Q: Which of the following would allow a digital signal processing filter to create a sharper filter response? A: More taps.
Notes: In DSP, increasing the number of taps in a finite impulse response (FIR) filter improves its ability to more precisely shape the passband and stopband. This results in a steeper transition between wanted and unwanted frequencies, which is useful in Amateur Extra-level signal filtering applications.

E7G05: Q: How can unwanted ringing and audio instability be prevented in an op-amp audio filter? A: Restrict both gain and Q.

E7G06: Q: What is the gain-bandwidth of an operational amplifier? A: The frequency at which the open-loop gain of the amplifier equals one.

E7G12: Q: What is an operational amplifier? A: A high-gain, direct-coupled differential amplifier with very high input impedance and very low output impedance.

### Group E7H - Oscillators, synthesizers, PLLs, and stability

E7H02: Q: What is a microphonic? A: Changes in oscillator frequency caused by mechanical vibration.

E7H03: Q: What is a phase-locked loop? A: An electronic servo loop consisting of a phase detector, a low-pass filter, a voltage-controlled oscillator, and a stable reference oscillator.

E7H04: Q: How is positive feedback supplied in a Colpitts oscillator? A: Through a capacitive divider.

E7H05: Q: How is positive feedback supplied in a Pierce oscillator? A: Through a quartz crystal.

E7H06: Q: Which of these functions can be performed by a phase-locked loop? A: Frequency synthesis and FM demodulation.

E7H07: Q: How can an oscillator’s microphonic responses be reduced? A: Mechanically isolate the oscillator circuitry from its enclosure.

E7H08: Q: Which of the following components can be used to reduce thermal drift in crystal oscillators? A: NP0 capacitors.

### Group E8A - Fourier concepts, RMS, and RF power
E8A01: Q: What technique shows that a square wave is made up of a sine wave and its odd harmonics? A: Fourier analysis.

E8A02: Q: Which of the following is a type of analog-to-digital conversion? A: Successive approximation.

E8A03: Q: Which of the following describes a signal in the time domain? A: Amplitude at different times.
Notes: Time-domain analysis looks at how the instantaneous amplitude of a signal changes over time. This is fundamental in amateur radio for understanding waveforms as they appear on instruments like oscilloscopes.
E8A04: Q: What is “dither” with respect to analog-to-digital converters? A: A small amount of noise added to the input signal to reduce quantization noise.
Notes: Adding a controlled noise signal (dither) to the analog input decorrelates the quantization error from the signal, making quantization noise less audible or less objectionable. This improves the effective resolution and perceived fidelity of the digitized signal in amateur radio and other communications systems.
E8A05: Q: What is the benefit of making voltage measurements with a true-RMS calculating meter? A: RMS is measured for both sinusoidal and non-sinusoidal signals.
Notes: In amateur radio work, many waveforms are not pure sine waves, so average-responding meters can give incorrect voltage readings. A true-RMS meter correctly measures the effective (heating) value of both sinusoidal and distorted or complex waveforms, which is essential for accurate power and signal measurements.
E8A06: Q: What is the approximate ratio of PEP-to-average power in an unprocessed single-sideband phone signal? A: 2.5 to 1.

E8A07: Q: What determines the PEP-to-average power ratio of an unprocessed single-sideband phone signal? A: Speech characteristics.
Notes: In an unprocessed SSB phone signal, the instantaneous peaks and average power levels follow the natural variations in human speech. Because of this, the inherent dynamics and waveform shape of the spoken audio directly set the ratio of PEP to average power for the signal.
E8A08: Q: Why are direct or flash conversion analog-to-digital converters used for a software defined radio? A: Very high speed allows digitizing high frequencies.

E8A09: Q: How many different input levels can be encoded by an analog-to-digital converter with 8-bit resolution? A: 256.

E8A10: Q: What is the purpose of a low-pass filter used at the output of a digital-to-analog converter? A: Remove spurious sampling artifacts from the output signal.
Notes: Digital-to-analog converters produce unwanted high-frequency components due to the discrete sampling process. A low-pass filter passes only the desired baseband or signal bandwidth and attenuates these spurious sampling artifacts so the analog output is clean for communication use.
E8A11: Q: Which of the following is a measure of the quality of an analog-to-digital converter? A: Total harmonic distortion.
Notes: Total harmonic distortion quantifies how much unwanted harmonic content is added to a signal by the conversion process, so lower distortion indicates a higher quality ADC. In Amateur Extra exam context, it is used as a key performance metric for evaluating how accurately an ADC preserves the original analog waveform.

### Group E8B - Modulation methods, index, and multiplexing
E8B01: Q: What is the modulation index of an FM signal? A: The ratio of frequency deviation to modulating signal frequency.
Notes: In FM, the modulation index quantifies how much the carrier frequency is shifted relative to the frequency of the audio or data signal causing the modulation. This ratio is fundamental for characterizing FM signals and determining their bandwidth in amateur radio systems.
E8B02: Q: How does the modulation index of a phase-modulated emission vary with RF carrier frequency? A: It does not depend on the RF carrier frequency.

E8B04: Q: What is the modulation index of an FM phone signal having a maximum carrier deviation of plus or minus 6 kHz if the highest modulating frequency is 2 kHz? A: 3.

E8B05: Q: What is the deviation ratio of an FM phone signal having a maximum frequency swing of plus or minus 5 kHz if the highest modulation frequency is 3 kHz? A: 1.67.

E8B06: Q: What is the deviation ratio of an FM phone signal having a maximum frequency swing of plus or minus 7.5 kHz if the highest modulation frequency is 3.5 kHz? A: 2.14.

E8B07: Q: Orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) is a technique used for which types of amateur communication? A: Digital modes.

E8B08: Q: What describes orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM)? A: A digital modulation technique using subcarriers at frequencies chosen to avoid intersymbol interference.
Notes: In OFDM, many closely spaced subcarriers are arranged so their spectra are mathematically orthogonal, which prevents overlapping symbols from interfering with each other. This orthogonality allows high data rates in channels subject to multipath and delay spread, minimizing intersymbol interference in amateur and other radio systems.
E8B09: Q: What is deviation ratio? A: The ratio of the maximum carrier frequency deviation to the highest audio modulating frequency.
Notes: In frequency modulation, deviation ratio quantifies how much the carrier can deviate in frequency relative to the highest audio frequency used to modulate it. This ratio is important for characterizing FM system performance and bandwidth in amateur radio communications.
E8B10: Q: What is frequency division multiplexing (FDM)? A: Dividing the transmitted signal into separate frequency bands that each carry a different data stream.

E8B11: Q: What is digital time division multiplexing? A: Two or more signals are arranged to share discrete time slots of a data transmission.

### Group E8C - Digital modes, bandwidth, error coding, constellations
E8C01: Q: What is Quadrature Amplitude Modulation or QAM? A: Transmission of data by modulating the amplitude of two carriers of the same frequency but 90 degrees out of phase.
Notes: In QAM, two orthogonal (90-degree phase-separated) carriers at the same frequency carry independent amplitude variations, allowing more information to be transmitted in the same bandwidth. E8C02: Q: What is the definition of symbol rate in a digital transmission? A: The rate at which the waveform changes to convey information.

E8C03: Q: Why should the phase of a PSK signal be changed at the zero crossing of the RF signal? A: To minimize bandwidth.

E8C04: Q: What technique minimizes the bandwidth of a PSK31 signal? A: Use of sinusoidal data pulses.
Notes: PSK31 uses shaped, sinusoidal data pulses to smooth phase transitions, which reduces abrupt changes that would otherwise create wider sidebands. This controlled shaping of the modulation envelope keeps the transmitted PSK31 signal very narrow in bandwidth for efficient HF operation.
E8C05: Q: What is the approximate bandwidth of a 13-WPM International Morse Code transmission? A: 52 Hz.

E8C06: Q: What is the bandwidth of an FT8 signal? A: 50 Hz.

E8C07: Q: What is the bandwidth of a 4,800-Hz frequency shift, 9,600-baud ASCII FM transmission? A: 15.36 kHz.

E8C08: Q: How does ARQ accomplish error correction? A: If errors are detected, a retransmission is requested.

E8C09: Q: Which digital code allows only one bit to change between sequential code values? A: Gray code.
Notes: Gray code is structured so that each adjacent code word differs by only one bit, which minimizes errors when transitioning between sequential digital values in radio and other digital systems. E8C10: Q: How can data rate be increased without increasing bandwidth? A: Using a more efficient digital code.

E8C11: Q: What is the relationship between symbol rate and baud? A: They are the same.

E8C12: Q: What factors affect the bandwidth of a transmitted CW signal? A: Keying speed and shape factor (rise and fall time).
Notes: Faster keying speeds and abrupt rise and fall times introduce more keying sidebands, increasing the CW signal’s occupied bandwidth. E8C13: Q: What is described by the constellation diagram of a QAM or QPSK signal? A: The possible phase and amplitude states for each symbol.

E8C14: Q: What type of addresses do nodes have in a mesh network? A: Internet Protocol (IP).

E8C15: Q: What technique do individual nodes use to form a mesh network? A: Discovery and link establishment protocols.

## SUBELEMENT E9 - Antennas and Feed Lines

Notes: Effective radiated power (ERP) is the calculated transmitter output power after including antenna gain and system losses, expressed as the equivalent power that would be radiated by a reference antenna. E9A05: Q: What does the term “ground gain” mean? A: An increase in signal strength from ground reflections in the environment of the antenna.

E9A06: Q: What is the effective radiated power (ERP) of a repeater station with 200 watts transmitter power output, 4 dB feed line loss, 3.2 dB duplexer loss, 0.8 dB circulator loss, and 10 dBd antenna gain? A: 317 watts.

### Group E9C - Wire antennas, arrays, and ground effects
E9C01: Q: What type of radiation pattern is created by two 1/4-wavelength vertical antennas spaced 1/2-wavelength apart and fed 180 degrees out of phase? A: A figure-eight oriented along the axis of the array.
Notes: Out-of-phase feeding of two verticals spaced 1/2-wavelength apart causes cancellation broadside to the array and reinforcement along its line, forming a figure-eight pattern along the array axis. This is a classic end-fire array configuration used to achieve directional gain in opposite directions along the antenna line.
E9C02: Q: What type of radiation pattern is created by two 1/4-wavelength vertical antennas spaced 1/4-wavelength apart and fed 90 degrees out of phase? A: Cardioid.
Notes: Feeding the two closely spaced verticals 90 degrees out of phase creates constructive interference in one main direction and destructive interference in the opposite direction, forming a cardioid pattern. This pattern is useful in amateur radio for directional coverage and front-to-back discrimination.
E9C03: Q: What type of radiation pattern is created by two 1/4-wavelength vertical antennas spaced 1/2-wavelength apart and fed in phase? A: A figure-eight broadside to the axis of the array.
Notes: Feeding two closely spaced verticals in phase causes their fields to add most strongly in the directions perpendicular to the line between them, forming a figure-eight pattern broadside to the array. This is a basic broadside array configuration used to increase gain in two opposite directions at right angles to the antenna line.
E9C04: Q: What happens to the radiation pattern of an unterminated long wire antenna as the wire length is increased? A: Additional lobes form with major lobes increasingly aligned with the axis of the antenna.
Notes: A longer unterminated long wire antenna supports more current maxima along its length, which creates more lobes in the radiation pattern. As the wire grows longer in wavelengths, the strongest radiation shifts so that the major lobes increasingly align with the axis of the wire, a behavior important in directional HF antenna design for the Amateur Extra exam.
E9C05: Q: What is the purpose of feeding an off-center-fed dipole (OCFD) between the center and one end instead of at the midpoint? A: To create a similar feed point impedance on multiple bands.
Notes: By moving the feed point off center, the antenna’s current distribution produces comparable feed point impedances at several harmonic frequencies. This allows one OCFD to operate efficiently on multiple amateur bands using a single feed line and matching system.
E9C06: Q: What is the effect of adding a terminating resistor to a rhombic or long-wire antenna? A: It changes the radiation pattern from bidirectional to unidirectional.
Notes: With a terminating resistor, energy that would otherwise be reflected back and form a reverse lobe is absorbed, so the antenna primarily radiates in one direction. This unidirectional pattern is useful in Amateur Extra level applications where a stronger signal is desired toward a specific distant region.
E9C07: Q: What is the approximate feed point impedance at the center of a two-wire half-wave folded dipole antenna? A: 300 ohms.
Notes: A two-wire half-wave folded dipole has about four times the feed point impedance of a standard half-wave dipole, giving approximately 300 ohms. This higher impedance is a characteristic result of the current distribution and parallel conductor arrangement in folded dipoles used in amateur radio antennas.
E9C08: Q: What is a folded dipole antenna? A: A half-wave dipole with an additional parallel wire connecting its two ends.
Notes: On the exam, a folded dipole is defined as a standard half-wave dipole whose two ends are joined by an added parallel conductor, forming a closed loop-like structure. This configuration changes the feed-point impedance and bandwidth characteristics while remaining a half-wave radiator.
E9C09: Q: Which of the following describes a G5RV antenna? A: A wire antenna center-fed through a specific length of open-wire line connected to a balun and coaxial feed line.
Notes: The G5RV is a multiband HF wire antenna that uses a precisely chosen length of open-wire matching section between the center of the wire and a balun that transitions to coaxial cable. This configuration provides impedance matching over several HF bands for Amateur Extra class operation.
E9C10: Q: Which of the following describes a Zepp antenna? A: An end-fed half-wavelength dipole.
Notes: A Zepp antenna is constructed as a half-wavelength dipole that is fed at one end instead of at the center. This end-fed configuration was originally used on airships (Zeppelins), giving the antenna its name in amateur radio practice.
E9C11: Q: How is the far-field elevation pattern of a vertically polarized antenna affected by being mounted over seawater versus soil? A: Radiation at low angles increases.
Notes: Seawater is a very good RF conductor, so it enhances ground reflection and improves efficiency for low-angle radiation. This results in stronger low-angle signals, which are especially useful for long-distance HF communication paths tested on the Amateur Extra exam.
E9C12: Q: Which of the following describes an extended double Zepp antenna? A: A center-fed 1.25-wavelength dipole antenna.
Notes: On the Amateur Extra exam, the extended double Zepp is defined as a dipole whose total length is 1.25 wavelengths and is fed at the center. This increased electrical length compared to a half-wave dipole provides additional gain in the main lobes of the radiation pattern along the wire’s axis.
E9C13: Q: How does the radiation pattern of a horizontally polarized antenna vary with increasing height above ground? A: The takeoff angle of the lowest elevation lobe decreases.
Notes: Raising a horizontally polarized antenna farther above ground causes its main radiation lobe to shift closer to the horizon, lowering the takeoff angle. This behavior is important for optimizing long-distance HF communication where low-angle radiation is desired.
E9C14: Q: How does the radiation pattern of a horizontally-polarized antenna mounted above a long slope compare with the same antenna mounted above flat ground? A: The main lobe takeoff angle decreases in the downhill direction.
Notes: The sloping terrain effectively increases the height of the antenna relative to the ground in the downhill direction, which lowers the radiation takeoff angle that is most useful for longer-distance HF communication. This terrain effect alters the radiation pattern even though the antenna itself is unchanged.

### Group E9D - Yagis, dishes, short antennas, and grounding
E9D01: Q: How much does the gain of an ideal parabolic reflector antenna increase when the operating frequency is doubled? A: 6 dB.
Notes: For an ideal parabolic reflector with fixed physical size, gain is proportional to the square of frequency. Doubling the frequency therefore results in a 4:1 power gain ratio, which corresponds to a 6 dB increase in gain in decibels (10 · log10(4) ≈ 6 dB).
E9D02: Q: How can two linearly polarized Yagi antennas be used to produce circular polarization? A: Arrange two Yagis on the same axis and perpendicular to each other with the driven elements at the same point on the boom and fed 90 degrees out of phase.
Notes: Circular polarization is created when two orthogonal linear fields of equal amplitude are combined with a 90-degree phase difference. Mounting the Yagis at right angles on the same boom point and feeding them 90 degrees out of phase provides the required orthogonal fields and phase relationship for circular polarization in amateur radio applications.
E9D03: Q: What is the most efficient location for a loading coil on an electrically short whip? A: Near the center of the vertical radiator.
Notes: Placing the loading coil near the center of the vertical radiator provides a better current distribution along the antenna, improving radiation efficiency for an electrically short whip. This configuration reduces losses compared to loading at less optimal points along the antenna.
E9D04: Q: Why should antenna loading coils have a high ratio of reactance to resistance? A: To maximize efficiency.
Notes: A high reactance-to-resistance ratio in a loading coil means less power is lost as heat in the coil and more power is radiated by the antenna. This improves the overall efficiency of the loaded antenna system, which is important in amateur radio operation.
E9D05: Q: Approximately how long is a Yagi’s driven element? A: 1/2 wavelength.
Notes: In a typical Yagi antenna used for amateur radio, the driven element is resonant at the operating frequency and is therefore made about one-half wavelength long. This length provides efficient radiation and reception while allowing proper coupling to the other elements in the array.
E9D06: Q: What happens to SWR bandwidth when one or more loading coils are used to resonate an electrically short antenna? A: It is decreased.
Notes: Loading coils increase the antenna’s Q, which narrows the range of frequencies over which it remains well matched. As a result, the standing wave ratio stays low over a smaller bandwidth.
E9D07: Q: What is an advantage of top loading an electrically short HF vertical antenna? A: Improved radiation efficiency.
Notes: Top loading increases the effective electrical length of a short vertical, allowing it to radiate more efficiently. This improves the antenna’s ability to convert transmitter power into radiated RF energy on HF bands.
E9D08: Q: What happens as the Q of an antenna increases? A: SWR bandwidth decreases.
Notes: Higher Q means the antenna system is more selective and resonates over a narrower range of frequencies. This increased selectivity causes the frequency range over which the SWR remains acceptably low (the SWR bandwidth) to become smaller.
E9D09: Q: What is the function of a loading coil in an electrically short antenna? A: To resonate the antenna by cancelling the capacitive reactance.
Notes: An electrically short antenna has capacitive reactance that prevents it from being resonant at the desired frequency. A loading coil adds inductive reactance that cancels this capacitive reactance, bringing the antenna to resonance for more efficient operation.
E9D10: Q: How does radiation resistance of a base-fed whip antenna change below its resonant frequency? A: Radiation resistance decreases.
Notes: At frequencies below resonance, the whip antenna becomes electrically shorter relative to the wavelength, which reduces its radiation resistance and makes it less efficient at radiating power. This behavior is a key consideration when tuning antennas for maximum radiation at the desired operating frequency.
E9D11: Q: Why do most two-element Yagis with normal spacing have a reflector instead of a director? A: Higher gain.
Notes: In a typical two-element Yagi with standard spacing, making the parasitic element a reflector maximizes the forward gain of the antenna. This higher gain improves signal strength and overall performance for amateur radio communications.
E9D12: Q: What is the purpose of making a Yagi’s parasitic elements either longer or shorter than resonance? A: Control of phase shift.
Notes: Adjusting the length of parasitic elements away from exact resonance changes their reactance, which in turn shifts the phase of the currents in those elements. This controlled phase shift is essential for forming the Yagi antenna’s directional radiation pattern used in amateur radio.

### Group E9E - Antenna impedance matching, phasing, and dividers
E9E01: Q: Which matching system for Yagi antennas requires the driven element to be insulated from the boom? A: Beta or hairpin.
Notes: In a beta or hairpin match, the driven element must be electrically isolated from the boom so the matching network can properly adjust the feedpoint impedance. This isolation ensures the match functions as designed to transform the antenna’s impedance to 50 ohms for the transmitter and feed line used in Amateur Extra operations.
E9E02: Q: What antenna matching system matches coaxial cable to an antenna by connecting the shield to the center of the antenna and the conductor a fraction of a wavelength to one side? A: Gamma match.
Notes: In amateur radio, the gamma match is specifically designed to feed an unbalanced coaxial line to a usually balanced antenna by tapping the driven element a fraction of a wavelength from its center. This configuration provides impedance transformation and matching without requiring a split driven element.
E9E03: Q: What matching system uses a short length of transmission line connected in parallel with the feed line at or near the feed point? A: Stub match.
Notes: In amateur radio, a stub match uses a short section of transmission line (the stub) in parallel with the main feed line to cancel unwanted reactance at the antenna feed point. This provides an impedance match between the feed line and antenna for efficient power transfer and reduced SWR.
E9E04: Q: What is the purpose of the series capacitor in a gamma match? A: To cancel unwanted inductive reactance.
Notes: The gamma match introduces inductive reactance that must be neutralized for efficient power transfer. The series capacitor provides equal and opposite reactance, bringing the system to a near purely resistive impedance at the operating frequency.
E9E05: Q: What Yagi driven element feed point impedance is required to use a beta or hairpin matching system? A: Capacitive (driven element electrically shorter than 1/2 wavelength).
Notes: Beta or hairpin matching uses an inductive reactance to cancel the capacitive reactance of a driven element that is electrically shorter than 1/2 wavelength. This brings the feed point impedance to a resistive value suitable for standard transmission lines in typical Amateur Extra Yagi designs.
E9E06: Q: Which of these transmission line impedances would be suitable for constructing a quarter-wave Q-section for matching a 100-ohm feed point impedance to a 50-ohm transmission line? A: 75 ohms.
Notes: For a quarter-wave transformer, the required characteristic impedance is the square root of the product of the two impedances being matched, and the square root of (100 ohms × 50 ohms) is 75 ohms. Using a 75-ohm line as a quarter-wave section will therefore match a 100-ohm load to a 50-ohm system in this application.
E9E07: Q: What parameter describes the interaction of a load and transmission line? A: Reflection coefficient.
Notes: The reflection coefficient quantifies how much of the signal is reflected back from the load due to impedance mismatch on a transmission line. In Amateur Extra exam context, it is the standard parameter used to describe the load-line interaction in terms of reflected versus incident power or voltage.
E9E08: Q: What is a use for a Wilkinson divider? A: To divide power equally between two 50-ohm loads while maintaining 50-ohm input impedance.
Notes: In amateur radio and RF design, a Wilkinson divider is a specialized power divider network that splits input power evenly into two matched 50-ohm outputs. Its design ensures the input still sees a 50-ohm impedance, which is important for proper system matching and minimizing reflections in 50-ohm systems.
E9E11: Q: What is the purpose of using multiple driven elements connected through phasing lines? A: To control the antenna’s radiation pattern.
Notes: By feeding multiple driven elements with specific phase relationships via phasing lines, the combined fields reinforce or cancel in chosen directions, shaping the radiation pattern. This technique lets amateurs direct more signal where it is needed and reduce radiation in unwanted directions, improving station performance under FCC Amateur Extra privileges.

### Group E9F - Transmission line types, loss, and velocity factor
E9F01: Q: What is the velocity factor of a transmission line? A: The velocity of a wave in the transmission line divided by the velocity of light in a vacuum.

E9F02: Q: Which of the following has the biggest effect on the velocity factor of a transmission line? A: The insulating dielectric material.
Notes: Velocity factor depends primarily on how the dielectric material between the conductors alters the speed of the electromagnetic wave compared to its speed in free space. Different insulating materials change the line’s effective permittivity, which directly sets the velocity factor used in antenna and feed line design calculations on the Amateur Extra exam.
E9F03: Q: Why is the electrical length of a coaxial cable longer than its physical length? A: Electromagnetic waves move more slowly in a coaxial cable than in air.
Notes: The dielectric material inside a coaxial cable reduces the wave propagation speed below the speed of radio waves in air or free space, which makes a given physical length correspond to a greater electrical length in terms of wavelength and phase. In antenna and transmission line calculations for the Amateur Extra exam, this slower velocity must be considered when determining resonant lengths and matching sections.
E9F04: Q: What impedance does a 1/2-wavelength transmission line present to an RF generator when the line is shorted at the far end? A: Very low impedance.

E9F05: Q: What is microstrip? A: Precision printed circuit conductors above a ground plane that provide constant impedance interconnects at microwave frequencies.
Notes: In the Amateur Extra exam context, microstrip refers to a specific transmission line structure on a printed circuit board that maintains a controlled impedance. It is widely used at microwave frequencies for predictable signal behavior and minimal reflections in RF circuits.
E9F06: Q: What is the approximate physical length of an air-insulated, parallel conductor transmission line that is electrically 1/2 wavelength long at 14.10 MHz? A: 10.6 meters.

E9F07: Q: How does parallel conductor transmission line compare to coaxial cable with a plastic dielectric? A: Lower loss.
Notes: Parallel conductor line operates with lower dielectric and conductor losses than typical plastic-dielectric coaxial cable, resulting in reduced signal attenuation. This makes it more efficient for carrying RF signals over a given distance, especially at higher frequencies or longer runs.
E9F08: Q: Which of the following is a significant difference between foam dielectric coaxial cable and solid dielectric coaxial cable, assuming all other parameters are the same? A: All these choices are correct:

  - Foam dielectric coaxial cable has lower loss per unit of length
  - Foam dielectric coaxial cable has higher velocity factor

E9F09: Q: What impedance does a 1/4-wavelength transmission line present to an RF generator when the line is shorted at the far end? A: Very high impedance.
Notes: A quarter-wavelength transmission line transforms a short circuit at its load end into a very high impedance at its input. This impedance-transforming property is used in RF design for impedance matching and creating resonant stubs.
E9F10: Q: What impedance does a 1/8-wavelength transmission line present to an RF generator when the line is shorted at the far end? A: An inductive reactance.

E9F11: Q: What impedance does a 1/8-wavelength transmission line present to an RF generator when the line is open at the far end? A: A capacitive reactance.
Notes: Because an open-circuited transmission line shorter than a quarter wavelength behaves like a capacitor, a 1/8-wavelength open line transforms the open circuit into a capacitive reactance at the generator. E9F12: Q: What impedance does a 1/4-wavelength transmission line present to an RF generator when the line is open at the far end? A: Very low impedance.
Notes: A quarter-wave transmission line transforms an open circuit at its far end into a virtual short circuit at the input. This impedance inversion property causes the RF generator to see a very low impedance at the line’s input when the far end is open.

E9H01: Q: When constructing a Beverage antenna, which of the following factors should be included in the design to achieve good performance at the desired frequency? A: It should be at least one wavelength long.

E9H07: Q: What is the function of a Beverage antenna’s termination resistor? A: Absorb signals from the reverse direction.

E9H08: Q: What is the function of a sense antenna? A: It modifies the pattern of a DF antenna to provide a null in only one direction.

E9H09: Q: What type of radiation pattern is created by a single-turn, terminated loop such as a pennant antenna? A: Cardioid.
Notes: A terminated loop like a pennant antenna is designed to be directional with a single deep null, which results in a cardioid-shaped radiation pattern. This cardioid pattern is useful on the Amateur Extra exam for understanding directional receiving antennas and noise rejection characteristics.
E9H10: Q: How can the output voltage of a multiple-turn receiving loop antenna be increased? A: By increasing the number of turns and/or the area enclosed by the loop.
Notes: A larger loop area and more turns intercept more of the passing magnetic field, which induces a higher voltage according to Faraday’s law of induction. In the Amateur Extra exam context, this emphasizes how loop geometry directly affects received signal strength.
E9H11: Q: What feature of a cardioid pattern antenna makes it useful for direction-finding antennas? A: A single null.
Notes: A single deep null lets the operator precisely identify the direction of minimum signal, which corresponds to a specific bearing for direction finding. This sharp null response improves accuracy when locating a signal source in Amateur Extra-level DF applications.


## SUBELEMENT E0 - Amateur Radio Safety
### Group E0A - RF safety, hazardous materials, and grounding
E0A01: Q: What is the primary function of an external earth connection or ground rod? A: Lightning charge dissipation.
Notes: A properly installed ground rod provides a low-impedance path for lightning energy to flow safely into the earth. This helps protect equipment and structures by directing lightning-induced charges away from the station circuitry.
E0A02: Q: When evaluating RF exposure levels from your station at a neighbor’s home, what must you do? A: Ensure signals from your station are less than the uncontrolled maximum permissible exposure (MPE) limits.
Notes: A neighbor’s home is considered an uncontrolled environment, so RF exposure there must not exceed the FCC’s uncontrolled MPE limits. This protects the general public, who are not expected to have RF safety training or control over the exposure conditions.
E0A03: Q: Over what range of frequencies are the FCC human body RF exposure limits most restrictive? A: 30 - 300 MHz.
Notes: In the 30 to 300 MHz range, the human body most efficiently absorbs RF energy, so the FCC sets the strictest exposure limits there to protect against excessive RF heating. Amateur Extra exam questions on RF safety use this range as the key reference for maximum exposure concern.
E0A04: Q: When evaluating a site with multiple transmitters operating at the same time, the operators and licensees of which transmitters are responsible for mitigating over-exposure situations? Notes: Below 300 MHz, the electric and magnetic components of RF fields can interact with the human body in different ways, so each must be limited separately. E0A07: Q: What is meant by “100% tie-off” regarding tower safety? A: At least one lanyard attached to the tower at all times.
Notes: 100% tie-off ensures a climber is continuously secured so a slip or loss of footing does not result in a fall. This continuous attachment is a fundamental safety requirement when working on towers in amateur radio and related services.
E0A08: Q: What does SAR measure? A: The rate at which RF energy is absorbed by the body.
Notes: SAR quantifies how much RF power per unit mass is taken up by body tissue, which is important for assessing human exposure to RF fields in amateur radio and other services. It is a key safety metric used in exposure standards and equipment certification.

E0A11: Q: To what should lanyards be attached while climbing? A: Tower legs.
Notes: Tower legs are the strongest and most secure structural members of the tower, providing a reliable attachment point for fall protection. Properly securing lanyards to tower legs reduces the risk of equipment failure or structural damage while climbing.
E0A12: Q: Where should a shock-absorbing lanyard be attached to a tower when working above ground? A: Above the climber’s head level.
Notes: Attaching the lanyard above head level minimizes the potential fall distance and reduces impact force if the climber slips. This positioning improves safety by allowing the shock absorber to function as intended within its rated limits.
